# Building the Navier–Stokes Equations

Water is the most familiar substance in human life. We drink it, swim in it, stir it
into coffee — and almost none of us can say a single true sentence about why it moves
the way it does.

In this article we'll build, piece by piece, the equation that governs it. You can see
that equation at work in the demonstration below — a stream flowing past a gray
cylinder, carrying <C k="dye">dye</C> that curls into a trail of eddies. The slider
changes just one property of the fluid; by the end of this article you'll know exactly
what that property is, and you'll have assembled every term of the equation running
this simulation:

<CylinderFlow variant="hero" />

The mathematics of fluids has a reputation, and some unfamiliar words will come up
along the way. You shouldn't feel pressured to memorize any of them — every quantity in
this article is color-coded, so <C k="vel">velocity</C> is always this blue,
<C k="dye">dye and drifting parcels</C> are always amber, and when something goes
physically wrong you will see it in <C k="div">violet</C>. The prose and the pictures
speak the same colors.

One honest contract before we start: the flows in this article are two dimensional,
and the fluid never compresses. Real water lives in three dimensions, but everything
we'll build survives the upgrade — the flat versions are simply the ones we can see
clearly. We'll begin by learning to see them.

## Seeing Flow

If you've ever watched a stream from a footbridge, you've noticed that the water
itself is nearly invisible — what you actually see is what the water *does*. Reeds
bend where the current drags on them, and petals drift wherever the surface carries
them. In the demonstration below, the slider controls the speed of time:

<FlowVis mode="creek" />

The <C k="visc">reeds</C> and the <C k="dye">petals</C> are reporting the same
underlying thing in two different ways, and both reports are worth keeping.

A reed is anchored in place. It tells us how fast the water moves *at its own spot*,
and it keeps telling us about that spot forever. We can distill this idea into a grid
of <C k="vel">arrows</C>, each fixed in place, each showing the direction and speed of
the flow right there — the longer the arrow, the faster the water:

<FlowVis mode="arrows" />

I'm drawing the <C k="vel">arrows</C> on a comfortable grid so they don't overlap, but
the spacing is a courtesy, not a fact about the water — between any two arrows the
flow has a velocity too, and we could draw an arrow at any point we liked.

A petal is the opposite kind of witness. It is anchored to nothing; it goes where the
water goes, and its path traces out a history of the current that carried it. We can
distill this idea too — little <C k="dye">markers</C>, light enough to instantly match
the flow around them, each dragging a ghostly trail of where it has been:

<FlowVis mode="markers" />

Notice that the two instruments genuinely disagree about what to report. An
<C k="vel">arrow</C> watches one place and sees many different parcels of water pass
through it. A <C k="dye">marker</C> watches one parcel of water and carries it through
many different places. Keep that distinction close; in a moment it will become the
heart of our first equation.

Sometimes we don't care about direction at all — only about how fast things are moving
everywhere at once. For that we can paint speed as color, brighter blue where the flow
runs faster:

<FlowVis mode="speed" />

And of course the instruments stack:

<FlowVis mode="combo" />

We can now see a flow three different ways. It's time to ask what, exactly, we are
looking at.

## A Field of Velocities

Water is made of molecules, and at a small enough scale the neat blue arrows dissolve
into chaos. In the demonstration below you're watching hundreds of particles rattling
around a box, colliding with the walls in a frenzy. The dashed box in the middle
averages the velocities of every particle inside it, and you can change its size with
the slider:

<ParticleBox mode="still" />

One disclosure about the staging: the big <C k="vel">blue arrow</C> is drawn several
times larger than any single particle's motion would justify, precisely so that you can
see how small the average is. And these particles bounce off the walls but pass through
each other — real molecules collide constantly, so I stand in for those collisions by
occasionally re-randomizing a particle's direction. The statistics are honest; the
choreography is simplified.

With a small averaging box the readout is jittery — a handful of particles is a noisy
committee. Make the box bigger and the average settles to almost exactly zero. All
that frantic motion, and the water as a whole goes nowhere. This is what still water
is: not stillness, but perfectly balanced commotion.

Now let's add a current. In the demonstration below the second slider superimposes a
drift on every particle — and notice the numbers before you touch it: the drift is
tiny compared to the thermal speed of the particles themselves.

<ParticleBox mode="wind" />

Watching individual particles, you can barely tell the difference between still water
and a strong current. However, the averaging box is not fooled. Even a drift far
smaller than the thermal chaos shows up immediately and unmistakably in the average.
Out of all the disorder, order emerges — and it emerges *per region*: average around
one spot and you get that spot's arrow; average around another and you get another.

That is what our arrow grid has been all along: every <C k="vel">arrow</C> is the
average velocity of the swarm of molecules near a point. This picture also lets us
leave the molecules behind — from here on, when we say the fluid has a
<C k="vel">velocity</C> at a point, we mean this average, and we never need to count
particles again.

Writing "the average velocity of the water near a given spot, at a given moment" will
get old quickly. Let's compress it: we'll write $\mathbf{u}(x, y, t)$ for the
<C k="vel">velocity</C> at position $(x, y)$ and time $t$ — the entire arrow grid,
every arrow at every point at every instant, folded into one symbol. A quantity that
has a value at every point like this is known as a *field*.

We can watch a field from the footbridge. The harder question is what the field looks
like to something *in* the water.

## Following a Parcel

Imagine a patch of <C k="dye">dye</C> sitting in a steady stream, and two instruments
measuring the dye's concentration. One is a probe bolted to the riverbed. The other
rides the current — a parcel of water we've tagged and released.

Here is the question that unlocks this whole section. The stream below is perfectly
steady: its velocity field and its dye patch never change in time. Nothing about the
picture depends on the clock.

<Predict
  question="If the field never changes in time, will the drifting parcel's readout change?"
  a="No — steady field, steady readings"
  b="Yes — the parcel's reading will change anyway"
>
  <ParcelProbe />
</Predict>

The <C k="wall">bolted probe</C> reads a flat line forever — its spot never changes.
But the <C k="dye">drifting parcel</C> sweeps into the dye patch, through its heart,
and out the far side, and its readout rises and falls even though *nothing in the
river ever changed*. The parcel experiences change not because the world changed, but
because it moved through a world that varies from place to place.

So "how fast is this quantity changing" is genuinely two different questions:

- at a fixed point: the field's own change in time, written $\partial q/\partial t$,
- riding the flow: the change a moving parcel feels.

The parcel's version has a name — it is known as the *material derivative*, written
$D/Dt$ — and the two questions differ by exactly one thing: motion through spatial
variation. A parcel moving with velocity $\mathbf{u}$ through a quantity that varies
in space at rate $\nabla q$ picks up change at the rate $\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla q$,
even in a frozen world:

$$\frac{Dq}{Dt} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\partial q}{\partial t}}_{\text{change at a point}} \;+\; \underbrace{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\,q}_{\text{change from being carried}}$$

Let's sanity-check the formula at its edges. If the parcel is at rest,
$\mathbf{u} = 0$, the carried term vanishes and the parcel agrees with the probe. If
the quantity is the same everywhere, $\nabla q = 0$, then moving through it changes
nothing — as it should. And in our steady stream, $\partial q/\partial t = 0$, so
*everything* the parcel felt came from the carried term. You just watched
$(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\,q$ with your own eyes.

Newton's law — force equals mass times acceleration — applies to *stuff*, and in a
fluid, the stuff is the parcels. Whatever equation we build will be a statement about
$D\mathbf{u}/Dt$, the acceleration a parcel actually feels. We have the left-hand side
of Navier–Stokes already; the rest of this article is a hunt for what belongs on the
right.

## Advection

The flow carries dye. Let's watch it do that in the demonstration below, where a
prescribed current sweeps an amber blob downstream:

<DyeCarry field="uniform" />

A uniform current just relocates the blob. Flows with structure do something more
interesting — they *deform* what they carry. A shearing current smears the blob into a
slanted streak:

<DyeCarry field="shear" />

And a vortex winds it into a spiral:

<DyeCarry field="vortex" />

This carrying-and-deforming is known as *advection*. For any carried quantity $q$, in
a world with no other physics, the parcel's value simply never changes —
$Dq/Dt = 0$ — which, unpacked with our new derivative, reads:

$$\frac{\partial q}{\partial t} \;=\; -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\,q$$

Here is the turn the whole subject pivots on. Velocity is itself carried by the flow.
A gust of fast water doesn't stay behind while the river moves on — the fast water *is*
water, and it goes where the water goes. So $q$ above can be $\mathbf{u}$ itself, and
the advection term becomes $(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\,\mathbf{u}$: **the field carrying
the field**. The equation feeds on its own output. This self-reference is known as
*nonlinearity*, and it is the single reason fluids can braid, swirl, and surprise —
and the reason no one has fully tamed the equation we're building.

Since we're computing all of this on a grid, honesty requires showing you how. The
demonstration below moves dye through a vortex with two different schemes. The first
is the obvious one: estimate the local slopes, nudge each cell accordingly. Watch what
it does — and notice the violet, which marks *negative dye*, a thing that cannot
exist:

<AdvectionSchemes />

The obvious update tears itself apart — not because our timestep is sloppy, but
because this scheme is unstable for transport no matter how small the step. The fix,
due to Jos Stam, is to ask the question backward: instead of pushing dye forward,
every cell asks *whose fluid arrives here?* — trace the velocity backward one step,
and take the answer waiting there (the dashed <C k="vel">blue arrow</C> in the second
mode asks exactly that question for one highlighted cell). Tracing backward is stable
at any timestep. Unfortunately, nothing is free: the price is a gentle blurring, and
you'll notice our simulations are slightly softer than reality on that account.

So the flow carries everything, including itself. But a real drop of dye in real water
does something none of our prescribed currents did — even in perfectly still water, a
sharp filament slowly fuzzes out. Something else is at work.

## Viscosity

Fluids can't keep secrets. Push on one part of a fluid and its neighbours find out
immediately: layers sliding past each other rub, and the rubbing pulls slow layers
along while holding fast ones back. That internal friction is
<C k="visc">viscosity</C>.

The demonstration below shows a *jet* — a band of fluid moving to the right,
sandwiched between still fluid, with a stationary wall at the top and bottom. Each
arrow is the velocity of one layer. Watch what happens to the sharp edges of the jet,
and drag the slider to change how strongly the layers grip each other:

<ViscosityDemo />

Two things are worth noticing. The sharp corners go first — friction attacks
*differences* between neighbours, and nowhere do neighbours disagree more than at an
edge. And the fluid never slips along the walls: real fluid sticks to solid surfaces,
a behavior known as the *no-slip condition*, and the walls quietly bleed momentum out
of the jet until nothing is left.

The same smoothing acts wherever two speeds meet. Fast water flowing over slow water
blends across the seam:

<ShearBlend />

And it is the assassin of every vortex. The swirl below survives for a long time in
water; in honey it dies while you watch:

<VortexDecay />

What you're watching in all three demonstrations is momentum spreading through the
fluid exactly the way heat spreads through a metal bar, and it obeys the same
equation:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} \;=\; \nu\,\nabla^2\mathbf{u}$$

Read the right-hand side as a measurement of disagreement: $\nabla^2\mathbf{u}$ at a
point is, up to a constant, how far that point's velocity sits from the average of its
neighbours'. Every instant, each parcel drifts toward its neighbourhood's average, and
the <C k="visc">viscosity</C> $\nu$ sets how fast. As a check, a perfectly linear
velocity profile has zero disagreement — every point is exactly the average of its
neighbours — and indeed such a profile doesn't change at all.

One warning about the word "thick." Viscosity is not density. Mercury is nearly
fourteen times denser than water, yet flows more easily; honey is lighter than mercury
and barely flows at all. At room temperature, water's viscosity is about 1 mPa·s, olive
oil's about 80, honey's about 5,000 — and air's is about 0.02, which is not zero, and
that small number will matter more than you'd think.

We now have two competing forces of character: advection stirs, viscosity smooths.
Everything a flow becomes is a negotiation between them.

## The Competition

Let's give the negotiation a stage. Below is our cylinder again, and one slider. At
the honey end, viscosity wins: the flow oozes around the obstacle and reunites,
orderly and symmetric. Drag toward the other end and advection takes over — the wake
begins to waver, then rolls up into the alternating street of eddies from the top of
this article, and beyond that it frays toward chaos:

<CylinderFlow variant="regime" />

The remarkable thing is that this entire zoo is governed by a single number. Double
the speed of the flow and halve the viscosity, and the wake looks the same. What
matters is only the *ratio* of stirring to smoothing — how much momentum the flow
carries versus how quickly viscosity can iron it flat. That ratio is known as the
*Reynolds number*:

$$Re \;=\; \frac{U L}{\nu}$$

where $U$ is the flow speed and $L$ is the size of the obstacle. (Getting from "the
ratio of two effects" to this exact expression takes a scaling argument we're waving
past; the further reading walks it properly.) Small $Re$: honey world. Large $Re$:
eddies and turbulence. The label on the demonstration above is this number,
order-of-magnitude, for our little grid.

The Reynolds number is why a bacterium and a jumbo jet inhabit different universes. A
swimming bacterium lives at $Re$ around $10^{-5}$ — to it, water is thicker than honey
is to us, and coasting is impossible; stop swimming and you halt within a fraction of
an atom's width. A 747's wing cruises at $Re$ around $10^8$. Your coffee, freshly
stirred, swirls at a few thousand — comfortably in eddy country, as the spiral off
your spoon confirms.

<Waypoint>
You now hold a velocity field $\mathbf{u}$, a derivative $D/Dt$ that rides it, the
term that stirs $-(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}$, the term that smooths
$\nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}$, and one number, $Re$, that referees the fight. What's missing
is the term that keeps fluid honest — because so far, nothing we've built stops fluid
from simply piling up.
</Waypoint>

## Pressure

Back to the molecules one last time. Their frenzy doesn't just average into velocity —
it also *pushes*. In the demonstration below, particles bombard a solid block from all
sides; every impact flashes red on its surface:

<ParticleBox mode="collide" />

Each individual impact is imperceptibly small, but there are staggeringly many of
them, and their combined, uninterrupted push on every surface is known as *pressure*.
In balance, the bombardment from the left cancels the bombardment from the right and
the block goes nowhere. Pressure only *moves* things where it is uneven — and here is
the part that matters for us: a parcel of fluid is bombarded by its neighbours in
exactly the same way. The fluid pushes on itself.

So the question is never "how large is the pressure" but "which way does it fall off."
Let's build some unevenness by hand. In the demonstration below you can drag a region
of <C k="pHi">high pressure</C> and a region of <C k="pLo">low pressure</C> around the
box; the shading shows the pressure everywhere:

<PressureLandscape mode="map" />

To see how quickly pressure changes from place to place, we can add contour lines —
the same device topographic maps use for altitude, and the analogy is worth taking
seriously: <C k="pHi">high pressure</C> is a hill, <C k="pLo">low pressure</C> is a
valley, and the closer the contour lines crowd together, the steeper the terrain:

<PressureLandscape mode="contours" />

A parcel of fluid in this landscape behaves like a marble on the terrain.

<Predict
  question="A parcel sits between a pressure hill ahead of it and a valley behind it. Which way does it accelerate?"
  a="Toward the hill — pressure attracts"
  b="Away from the hill, toward the valley"
>
  <PressureLandscape mode="parcels" />
</Predict>

The <C k="dye">parcels</C> roll off the <C k="pHi">hills</C> and gather speed toward
the <C k="pLo">valleys</C> — always down the steepest slope, faster where the slope is
steeper. The mathematical name for "the direction and steepness of the slope" is the
*gradient*, written $\nabla p$, and the force on a parcel points down it:

$$\text{pressure force on a parcel} \;=\; -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p$$

The minus sign is the rolling-downhill; $\rho$, the density, converts push into
acceleration — the same shove moves light fluid more than heavy fluid. As a check: on
flat terrain, $\nabla p = 0$ and pressure moves nothing, no matter how large it is.
Deep-sea creatures live their whole lives under crushing pressure that pushes them
nowhere.

We now have three influences on a parcel. It is time to discover why we can't just
add them up and declare victory.

## The Broken Fluid

Let's try it. The demonstration below runs our cylinder channel with carrying and
smoothing switched on, and nothing else — no pressure, no constraint. Physics as we've
built it so far. The <C k="div">violet</C> glow marks places where fluid is being
created or destroyed:

<CylinderFlow variant="broken" />

This is clearly wrong! Dye piles up against the cylinder and *soaks into it*; the
stream stacks water into heaps as if it were sand. Real water does not pile up in a
corner of the cup — push water into a spot that's already full and it pushes back,
instantly, and goes around.

What we've violated is easier to see with a counting argument. Draw any closed loop in
a real flow and tally the water crossing it — in through some parts, out through
others. Since water doesn't compress and doesn't vanish, the ins must exactly cancel
the outs, for *any* loop, *anywhere*. See for yourself; drag the loop around and
resize it:

<DivergenceLoop withSource={false} />

However you place the loop, the net flux reads zero. Now let's plant a hidden spring —
a spot that manufactures new fluid out of nothing. Catch it inside the loop:

<DivergenceLoop withSource={true} />

The loop catches a stubborn surplus, and the <C k="div">violet</C> shading marks the
scene of the crime. The local, per-point version of this counting rule is known as the
*divergence*, written $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}$ — roughly, the net outflow from a tiny
neighbourhood. For real, incompressible fluid the law is stark:

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} \;=\; 0$$

everywhere, always. This is not a suggestion the flow gradually satisfies; it holds at
every instant. Our broken simulation glows violet precisely where its divergence is
nonzero. Something must cancel divergence the moment it tries to appear — and we've
already met the only actor fast enough.

## Pressure, the Instant Fixer

You take the controls first. The demonstration below has a spring pumping fluid
outward from one spot — <C k="div">violet crime</C> everywhere around it. The slider
raises a <C k="pHi">pressure hill</C> centered on the same spot. The hill's slope
pushes fluid away; your job is to cancel the crime:

<PressureFix />

Too low a hill, and fluid keeps piling up. Too high, and you've overcorrected — the
hill hollows the spot out, a crime in the other direction. There is exactly one
height at which the books balance. You have just done, by hand and by eye, what real
pressure does everywhere in every fluid, continuously, without a hand on any slider:
where fluid begins to pile, pressure rises and pushes it apart; where fluid begins to
thin, pressure falls and draws it back. Any imbalance corrects itself before it can
grow.

There's a beautiful piece of mathematics underneath your slider-work. Any smooth flow
field can be split into two ingredients — a part that only swirls (zero divergence)
and a part that only piles (all of the divergence, and it is the gradient of
something). The demonstration below is built from exactly those two ingredients, and
you can look at each alone:

<HelmholtzSplit />

That split is known as the *Helmholtz decomposition* — shown here, proven in the
further reading. It tells us the fix always exists: whatever mess a flow proposes, its
piling part is the gradient of some hill-and-valley landscape, so *some* pressure
field's push can cancel it exactly, leaving only honest swirl behind.

Finding that pressure field means solving a puzzle where every point's pressure
depends on its neighbours'. Demanding that the corrected flow have zero divergence
turns out to require, in the discrete world of our grid:

$$\nabla^2 p \;=\; \frac{\rho}{\Delta t}\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}^*$$

— where $\mathbf{u}^*$ is the flow *before* correction. You don't need to unpack this
one symbol by symbol; read it as "the pressure landscape whose disagreement-with-
neighbours matches the crime." An equation of this shape is known as a *Poisson
equation*, and a computer solves it by patient negotiation: every cell repeatedly
updates its pressure toward what its four neighbours (and the local crime) suggest.
Scrub through that negotiation yourself:

<JacobiRelax />

At zero sweeps: all <C k="div">crime</C>, no pressure. A few dozen sweeps of
neighbourly negotiation later, the <C k="pHi">hill</C> has risen and the crime is
gone. These sweeps are known as *Jacobi iterations*, and our simulations run a few
dozen of them every frame, at every one of ten thousand cells.

One last thing deserves saying plainly, because it is the strangest fact in this
article. Pressure has no equation of its own — no $\partial p/\partial t$ anywhere.
It is not a substance with momentum and history. It is whatever field, at each
instant, keeps $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$ true. Ask a fluid "why this pressure?" and
the only answer is: *because that is what it took*.

<Waypoint>
You now hold the constraint $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$, and its enforcer — a pressure
field conjured fresh at every instant by a Poisson solve. Nothing more is missing.
</Waypoint>

## Assembling Navier–Stokes

Newton, for a parcel of fluid: mass times acceleration equals the sum of forces. We
have the acceleration a parcel feels, and every force. Written out:

$$\underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t}}_{\text{change at a point}} \;=\; \underbrace{-(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\,\mathbf{u}}_{\text{the flow carries itself}} \;-\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p}_{\text{downhill from pressure}} \;+\; \underbrace{\nu\,\nabla^2\mathbf{u}}_{\text{neighbours drag on neighbours}} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathbf{f}}_{\text{outside pushes}} \qquad \text{with} \qquad \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$$

These are the incompressible Navier–Stokes equations — Claude-Louis Navier and George
Gabriel Stokes arrived at them a century and a half before anyone could compute with
them. There is no term in that line you have not personally dragged a slider through.
($\mathbf{f}$ is simply any push from outside — gravity, a propeller, your spoon.)

And because every term is now a friend, we can do something textbooks can't: perform
surgery on the equation while it runs. The demonstration below is our channel with
each term on its own switch — this figure earns its extra controls:

<TermToggle />

Switch off the carrying term and you enter the Stokes world: honey physics, every
disturbance ironed flat, stately and orderly — this is the bacterium's whole life.
Switch off viscosity instead and you get the Euler world, sharp filaments that refuse
to blur (in principle forever; in practice our backward-tracing scheme sneaks in a
little blur of its own, as confessed earlier). Switch off pressure and you recognize
the violet catastrophe of the broken fluid — now as a diagnosis rather than a mystery.

One confession completes the ceremony. Whether solutions of these equations, in three
dimensions, always remain smooth — or can spontaneously concentrate infinite velocity
into a point — is not known. Not "hard": *unknown*. It is one of the seven Clay
Millennium Prize Problems, and the equation you just assembled with sliders carries a
standing million-dollar bounty for whoever settles it.

## Running the Equation

An equation nobody can solve by hand can still be *walked* by a machine, one small
step at a time. Everything you've watched in this article runs on a scheme called
*Stable Fluids*, published by Jos Stam in 1999, and you already know all four of its
moves — each one is a section of this article. Every frame, for every cell: add the
outside pushes, carry the field backward-tracing style, smooth it by viscosity, then
solve the pressure puzzle and cancel the crime.

The demonstration below lets you watch each move in isolation, running repeatedly on
the same channel. Carrying alone stretches and folds but lets crime accumulate;
smoothing alone melts everything toward stillness; the pressure step alone hunts
divergence and touches nothing else:

<SolverXray />

Chain the four moves and you get water. And with that, the demonstration from the top
of this article surrenders its mystery. Here it is again — the same solver, the same
street of eddies. The slider was the Reynolds number all along. This time you can also
stir it with your pointer:

<CylinderFlow variant="finale" />

Fair warnings, so you know exactly what you're holding: the backward-tracing step
blurs fine detail, so our flows are a touch more viscous than the dial admits; true
turbulence cascades down to scales far below any grid; and a plane channel on ten
thousand cells is not the ocean. I would advise against using this solver to design a
submarine. But every term in it is honest, and every one of them is yours now.

## Where Waves Live

This is a wave-physics course, and we've spent the whole first lesson on an equation
with no waves in it. That was the point: Navier–Stokes is the parent, and the waves
are its children.

Take still water and disturb it *slightly*. With disturbances so small, the
self-carrying term — small velocity times small variation — becomes negligible
squared-smallness, and the fearsome nonlinear equation goes docile and linear. Allow
the fluid the tiniest bit of compression (our one contract, relaxed for a moment) and
the leftover physics is pressure pushing on inertia: a disturbance in pressure shoves
the fluid ahead of it, which compresses the next region, which shoves again. The
disturbance *propagates*:

<RippleWaves mode="sound" />

That traveling negotiation between <C k="pHi">pressure</C> and inertia is sound. The
same linearizing move at a free water surface, with gravity as the restoring push
instead, gives water waves — and notice the amber floats: they bob nearly in place
while the shape sweeps past. The wave travels; the water mostly doesn't:

<RippleWaves mode="surface" />

Both obey the *wave equation* — the small, well-behaved child of the giant we built
today, and the equation this course now sets out to explore. Everything ahead of us —
strings, sound, light's analogies, water — lives inside mathematics you have already
touched.

## Further Reading and Watching

Lorena Barba's *12 Steps to Navier–Stokes* is the finest computational on-ramp there
is: twelve Jupyter notebooks that rebuild this article's mathematics with your own
hands on the keyboard, ending at a working solver. If this lesson made you want to
*compute*, go there next.

Jos Stam's paper *Real-Time Fluid Dynamics for Games* (2003) is the friendliest
description of the exact solver running our demonstrations — a rare paper you can read
with one hand and implement with the other. His original *Stable Fluids* (1999) is the
formal version.

Robert Bridson's *Fluid Simulation for Computer Graphics* is the book behind nearly
every fluid you've seen in a film. It treats the pressure projection with a care and
clarity no online tutorial matches.

Bartosz Ciechanowski's *Airfoil* is the finest interactive exposition of fluid
intuition ever put on the web, and this lesson is openly in conversation with it — he
builds the physical feel for pressure and viscosity with a patience I can only point
at, and stops exactly where this article begins: at the equations.

And braintruffle's video series *Mastering fluid dynamics* climbs from molecules to
continuum with visuals that justify the watch time on their own.

## Final Words

The next time you stir your coffee, watch the spiral peel off the spoon. That curl is
the carrying term feeding on itself; the smooth fading of it afterward is $\nu$ at
work; and the reason the coffee never piles up against the cup's wall is a pressure
field being conjured and dissolved, instant by instant, to keep a divergence of
exactly zero — the same silent bookkeeping running in every faucet, every river, and
every breath of wind you've ever felt.

I find it steadying that beneath all that motion sits one line of mathematics — and
that the line is still, in the deepest sense, open. Understanding it hasn't drained
the water of its magic. You now know what the swirl *is*, and it is more astonishing,
not less: an unsolved equation, performing itself flawlessly, in your cup.
