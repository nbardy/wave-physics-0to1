# The History of Navier–Stokes

> …a singular paradox which I leave to future Geometers to elucidate.
>
> — Jean le Rond d'Alembert, 1768, on his proof that an ideal fluid exerts no drag

The equation you assembled in lesson 01 took humanity a hundred and sixty years to
write. It was discovered five separate times by five men who mostly refused to cite
each other, and it is named after two of them — a French bridge engineer and an
Irish-born Cambridge professor — who never met. Its central paradox stood for 152
years and fell in a ten-minute conference talk. Whether its solutions always behave
is still open, and worth a million dollars. This lesson walks the same cylinder,
the same stream, and the same terms as lesson 01 — in the order history built them,
with the names and birthdays attached.
