# Fiber Bundles, the Universal Medium

> What if waves are among the most beautiful & powerful things in the world, and
> mathematicians generalized diff calculus so that there was a universal medium for
> all waves, yet didn't tell anyone outside a few theoretical physicists?
> That would be fiber bundles. Just for openers.
>
> — [Eric Weinstein](https://twitter.com/ericrweinstein)

Every wave you have ever met waved *in something* — water, air, a string, the fluid of
the previous lesson. The light reaching your eye right now waves in nothing that has
ever been found.

The nineteenth century named the missing substance the *aether* and hunted it with the
most sensitive instrument it knew how to build; in 1887 Michelson and Morley measured,
to their lasting dismay, nothing at all. The resolution taught in school — "fields
don't need a medium" — is a name, not an answer. This article builds the answer. In
the demonstration below, the top pane is the picture of light you have seen before:

by. The bottom pane is the same wave, drawn as a pulse running down a row of clocks.

## A Wave Is a Choice at Every Point

Let's begin with the wave we already own. Below is the previous lesson's string,
carrying a displacement wave — the most familiar object in this article, about to be
looked at in an unfamiliar way:

<StringSection stage="string" />

Let's make the space of possibilities visible: over every point of the line, a faint
vertical rail — the private space of values that one point owns. The string's height
at a point is a position on that point's rail:

<StringSection stage="rails" />

I draw a dozen rails so the picture stays readable; in truth a rail stands over
every point, a continuous fluid of them.

Seen this way, a wave stops being a shape and becomes a *decision*: at every point,
one choice from that point's rail. The demonstration below draws the same wave as its
threading — one amber bead per rail, the wave being the run of beads:

<StringSection stage="section" />

The rail over a point is known as a *fiber* — the menu of values on offer there. The
threading — one choice per fiber, varying smoothly — is known as a *section*.

One more piece of anatomy: the strip all the rails sweep out together, the space
every possible graph lives inside, is known as the *total space*:

<StringSection stage="strip" />

Now we can reread the previous lesson's notation with new eyes. We wrote $y(x,t)$
for the string's height, and the symbol was carrying this structure all along:
$y(x,t)$ is the choice made in the fiber over $x$ at time $t$ — a moving section.
Nothing about the wave ever leaves the strip. However,
the strip is hiding a choice that nothing in the string ever exposed — and to flush
it out, we have to bend the strip.

## Twist You Cannot Comb Away

Take the strip of rails and bend it into a loop, so the line closes on itself. At the
seam where the ends meet, we face a decision nothing has forced on us before: how the
last rail glues to the first. The unassuming choice is straight across, top to top.
The result is the untwisted band below, and it comes with a game — you can drag to
comb the amber section all the way around, keeping it away from zero the whole way:

<MobiusComb kind="cylinder" />

Nothing resists you; any nonzero height combs smoothly around and meets itself. Now
the other gluing: give the strip one half-twist before joining the ends, so top glues
to bottom. At every individual point this band is indistinguishable from the last
one — same rails, same neighborhoods, same game:

<MobiusComb kind="mobius" />

Unfortunately, the game is now unwinnable. Comb however you like: the section is
forced through zero, every single time, and the meter catches the crossing. This
shape is known as the *Möbius band*, and your failure is not clumsiness — it is a
theorem. The twist lives in no particular place, since every local patch is innocent;
the whole is different in kind, and no smooth re-combing removes it.

This is what makes a bundle a bundle: *locally a product, glued by choices* — the
gluing is data with consequences, not pedantry. Keep this flip in your pocket; near
the end of the article we will need to know exactly what kind of twist it is.

Notice what else was never forced on us: the shape of the fiber itself. Nothing says
the thing standing over each point must be a segment. A circle can stand over a point
just as well.

## The Phase Clock

You have held that fiber in your hands. Take a rope by one end and whirl it, and each
point of the rope travels a circle around the line it would otherwise rest along —
the corkscrew wave of a swung jump rope, known to physics as *circular polarization*:

<RopeCircle stage="rope" />

One confession at the moment of borrowing, because this is a real debt: the rope is a
loaner. Nature's own row of circles — the one this article is finally about — has no
visible needle and no ambient "up"; what actually stands in its fibers is a debt I
will pay before we are done. For now, the rope keeps everything drawable.

Let's extract the structure: over each point of the line, a clock face — the circle
of directions that point's piece of rope can occupy — and the wave is no longer a
height but an angle:

<RopeCircle stage="extract" />

We'll write $\theta(x,t)$ for that angle: a circle-valued section. Its total space, a
line of circle fibers, is a cylinder — with the roles swapped since the band game:
there the base was a circle and the fibers were segments; here the base is a line and
the fiber is the circle. Only this total space gets the name *cylinder*; the
untwisted band keeps its own name.

Here, then, is the protagonist of the rest of this article: the clock-row, with a
graph pane beneath it plotting $\theta(x)$ — the bundle picture above, its reading
below, and the mapping between the two panes is the lesson. A steady wave runs along
it, and the figure has gained a small meter:

<RopeCircle stage="clocks" />

The meter is a readout, not a control. It computes the string's energy recipe applied
to the clocks — rotation rate squared plus twist rate squared,
$\int [(\partial\theta/\partial t)^2 + c^2 (\partial\theta/\partial x)^2]\,dx$ — and
holds a steady 1.00 while the wave runs. To write a wave equation for the clocks we
will need that twist rate $\partial\theta/\partial x$, the analog of the string's
slope. And waiting inside that innocent derivative is a problem the string never had.

## The Prankster's Derivative

Recall the seam: the gluing of neighboring fibers was a *choice*. The same is true
all along the line, not just where ends meet — somebody has to decide how one clock's
face aligns with the next one's, which direction here counts as "the same direction"
there. For the string, nobody ever had to choose. That is worth saying plainly now,
because the reason will matter soon.

There is a household precedent. Before railway time, every town set noon by its own
sun; comparing clocks across towns required a convention, and no convention was more
correct than another. The anchor carries one honest disanalogy, and it cuts our way:
the towns at least had a shared sky to appeal to — our clocks, we will see, have
nothing of the kind.

Our rope, however, hangs in a room whose "up" every point shares — so its clock

the visible motion of the rope, beside *formula-energy* — the previous meter,

string's energy formula applied to the plotted $\theta$, the only energy we know how

<RegaugeBrush stage="pre" />

Now the crime. You hold a *regauge brush*: stroking it across the row smoothly
rotates each clock's zero-mark by an angle <C k="gauge">$\alpha(x)$</C> of your
choosing — repainting the dials, touching nothing else. Commit before you brush:

The rope-meter sits still. The formula-meter jumps anyway. Nothing physical changed —
the rope's motion, kept visible in the top pane, never varies under your brush — yet
the plotted $\theta(x)$ writhes, its slope changes everywhere, and every number built
from that slope changes with it. What the brush performs is a relabeling:

$$\theta \;\to\; \theta + \alpha(x)$$

One boundary check worth running: a constant <C k="gauge">$\alpha$</C> — every
zero-mark turned by the same amount — leaves every slope alone. Only a prank that
*varies from place to place* breaks the derivative, and place-varying pranks are
exactly what the freedom allows. So the verdict is flat: *the ordinary derivative of
a section is not a fact about the wave; it is a fact about our labels.* The
rope-meter stayed honest only because its recipe never consulted the labels — it
watched a rope hanging in a room whose "up" every point shares. Unfortunately, every
wave equation ever written is built from derivatives.

For the rope, then, the prankster's freedom is a bookkeeping nuisance. Watch what
remains when the bookkeeping props are taken away:

<RegaugeBrush stage="stripped" />

The room and the rope fade, leaving clock faces alone in the dark. Nature's clocks,
I'll confess now and pay in full later, come with no room and no visible needle — no
experiment has ever read one clock's absolute angle; only comparisons between clocks
have ever been measured. What we lack — and what nature must somehow also supply — is
a rule for carrying a needle from one clock to the next.

## The Transport Rule

Let's build the missing rule by hand, as data. In the demonstration below, a needle
is carried along the line, rotating as it goes by a prescribed amount per step — the
prescription being a field <C k="conn">$A(x)$</C>, drawn as its own blue curve. The
slider scales the prescription; at zero, the needle is carried rigidly, in the
ordinary way:

<TransportNeedle />

That blue curve is a *transport rule*: an answer, at every point, to which direction
on the next fiber counts as the same direction — precisely the statement the
prankster attacked, but now carried as explicit data rather than inherited from the
scenery.

Time to put it to work. Below, a wave has been mangled by a brushstroke of fixed
shape — visibly the same bump the last section's brush applied — and you hold one
slider: the amplitude of a transport rule with that same bump shape. The graph pane
gains a second trace, the wave as *read through your rule* — a needle carried by
<C k="conn">$A$</C>, plotted as its disagreement with the section — with a ghost of
the pre-mangle wave as the standing target, and a small meter scoring the residual:

<ConnectionTuner mode="stage1" />

Too little and the reading still writhes; too much and you have overcorrected.
Somewhere between, the meter locks: read through the right rule, the mangled wave is
calm. Notice the one thing that never moves while you tune — the raw $\theta$ trace.
The rule repairs the *reading*, not the labels.

Now the honest version, with a mangling built from several strokes, and three handles
on your rule:

<ConnectionTuner mode="stage2" />

After some trial and error you may get pretty close. But a real brush has infinitely
many handles, and matching it stroke for stroke by hand is a hopeless occupation.
Thankfully, the number your hand has been driving to zero can be named once and for
all, and its name is the whole rescue. It is the *covariant derivative*:

$$D\theta \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}}_{\text{measured twist}} \;-\; \underbrace{A}_{\text{expected twist}}$$

— the twist the labels report, minus the twist the <C k="conn">rule</C> expected.
Boundary check first: with $A = 0$ everywhere, $D$ is the ordinary derivative, and
everything from the previous lesson survives untouched.

The rescue then completes itself under your own hand. In the demonstration below you
can brush a fresh <C k="gauge">$\alpha$</C> over the wave you just repaired — and the
blue curve absorbs the stroke while the $D\theta$ plot sits perfectly still; the
shift <C k="conn">$A$</C> picks up matches the $\partial\alpha$ of your brushstroke,
stroke for stroke. And recall the meter that lied: rebuilt from $D\theta$ in place of
$\partial\theta$, it now holds still under anything you can paint:

<ConnectionTuner mode="law" />

The law your hand just failed to break, written down:

$$\theta \;\to\; \theta + \alpha, \qquad A \;\to\; A + \frac{\partial\alpha}{\partial x}, \qquad D\theta \;\to\; D\theta$$

The prankster relabels; the <C k="conn">rule</C> compensates; every quantity built
from $D$ means something again. The rebuilt meter is the first physically meaningful
number our repaired calculus has bought us — the meter that lied now tells the truth.

With it comes a sharper truth about the string. "Just use the room's up" was never an
*alternative* to choosing a transport rule; it was choosing one — the flat rule,
$A = 0$, handed to us free by the room the string is embedded in. That is why the
string and the rope never had this problem: their connection came bundled with the
scenery. Nature's clocks come with no room, so their transport rule must be bought as
physical data. The epigraph's first clause is hereby paid in full: this is
*differential calculus, generalized*, and the mathematicians' name for the whole
package is a *connection*.

History ran this exact failure chain before us. Hermann Weyl built the first gauge
theory in 1918 with the wrong fiber — he let the *length scale* be the transported
thing, with electromagnetism as its rule — and Einstein pointed out that atoms would
then remember their histories, which their spectral lines visibly don't. The quantum
phase repaired the theory in 1929: right structure, wrong fiber, eleven years apart.
Even the inventor's naive version broke first.

<Waypoint>
You now hold fibers and the sections threading them; the prankster who relabels them
at will; and a transport rule $A$ whose derivative $D\theta$ makes calculus mean
something again — governed by one law: whatever the brush does to $\theta$, $A$
absorbs.
</Waypoint>

One suspicion before we celebrate — and on the line it is not paranoia but a theorem:
every <C k="conn">$A$</C> we have met was written by our own brush, and every $A$ on
a line can be erased by one — a diary of brushstrokes, nothing more. What rescues $A$
from bookkeeping is the plane. Where the needle has many ways to travel, the diary
theory becomes testable — and the ways need not agree.

## Around a Loop

The base grows: a plane of clocks. One debt is due at the door — with many directions
to step, the needle needs a rotation-per-step for each direction it can travel, so
<C k="conn">$A$</C> upgrades from a scalar to a pair $(A_x, A_y)$, one number per
direction of step. In the demonstration below, a needle travels from P to Q twice,
along two different routes, transported by the same rule both times:

<HolonomyLoop mode="two-paths" />

The arrivals disagree. Neither route did anything wrong — every step obeyed the
rule — yet the needles arrive pointing differently. On a line there was only one way
to travel, and disagreement had no room to exist; that is exactly why the line could
prove nothing.

Two paths that disagree from P to Q are the same thing as one closed loop that brings
a needle home rotated — out along one route, back along the other. So the sharp
question is about loops, and it deserves a commitment:

The loop around the core brings its needle home rotated; the other returns exactly
as it left. A needle rotated by a round trip is a per-loop fact about the rule, and
the phenomenon is known as *holonomy*. You can hunt it yourself below — the loop
keeps a fixed size, you can drag it anywhere on the plane, and the meter totals the
rotation-per-step around it, written $\oint A$:

<HolonomyLoop mode="explore" />

Notice what the meter cares about. Position changes nothing while the core stays
outside; only *enclosure* counts. Keep that — it is the entire mystery of the sealed
core, waiting for us.

The planet under you has been performing this trick on travelers forever. Below, the
same amber needle lies flat on a globe and is carried along a triangle — along the
equator, up to the pole, back down to the start — kept as parallel as the ground
permits at every step. You can drag to orbit, and walk the triangle:

<GlobeTransport />

The needle comes home turned by a quarter turn, having never once been rotated on
purpose. An imperfect, but convenient analogy — and the imperfection is the important
part: on the globe, the sphere's own shape writes the transport rule; on our plane of
clocks the floor is flat, and a *field* writes it. Same phenomenon, different author.
Hold onto that distinction; the sealed core will redeem it.

Holonomy, so far, is a per-loop fact. Shrink the loop and it becomes a per-area
density — a number waiting for a name.

## Curvature

Let's do the shrinking with the meters running. The loop below sits at a fixed
position; the slider is its radius, and two meters run live — the rotation collected
around the loop, and the running total of a violet density over the area inside:

<HolonomyLoop mode="shrink" />

Halve the radius and the collected rotation drops fourfold: the loop's take is
proportional to its *area*. So the honest local object is rotation per area — a
density, written <C k="curv">$F$</C> — and on our plane it has an exact recipe, along
with the equality your two meters have been agreeing about at every radius:

$$F \;=\; \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial y}, \qquad \oint A \;=\; \iint F$$

Mathematics files the loop-total-equals-area-total statement under the name of
Stokes; you have just watched it rather than cited it. And <C k="curv">$F$</C> earns
its own picture — a violet map over the plane, bright where loops collect rotation
fastest:

<HolonomyLoop mode="field" />

Now the boundary check, and it doubles as the verdict on the suspicion we brought
from the line. Run the loop meter over a pure diary — a rule $A = \partial\alpha$
that some brushstroke alone could have written:

<HolonomyLoop mode="gauge-check" />

Zero. Every loop, every position, every radius: zero. The brush can move
<C k="conn">$A$</C> around, but it cannot create or destroy <C k="curv">$F$</C> — so
a diary scores zero on every loop, and anything a loop meter reads is *not diary*.

One sentence to keep two twists apart, as promised. The Möbius flip is *topology*:
discrete, all-or-nothing, surviving any smooth re-combing. Holonomy is *geometry*:
continuous, tunable, a per-area density. Different kinds of twist — and the case that
needs the distinction is two sections away from paying for it.

What we now hold is a field you cannot gauge away, measured in loops. Physics has a
name for it.

## The Wave in the Connection

Unfreeze <C k="conn">$A$</C>. Give the connection a life of its own — the simplest
possible one: it costs energy for the rule to curve, and it costs energy for the rule
to change. This is the one thing in this article we add by hand, and the confession
deserves full volume: the repair was forced on us, but its dynamics is a postulate —
the simplest on offer. The postulate carries a rider, stated here and not derived:
the simplest dynamics happens to play no favorites among uniformly moving observers.
The string's wave equation looks identical on paper, but it holds in the frame of the
string's own material; here there is no material underneath, so that reason for a
preferred frame is gone — and that no other reason sneaks in is special relativity's
fact, which this article states and does not earn.

Everything after that confession is consequence, and we can watch the first one on
household hardware: a row of compass needles, and a bar magnet you can wiggle at one
end:

<ConnectionWave mode="compass" />

What travels down the row is not any needle, and not a substance between them — it is
the *redefinition of where each needle wants to rest*, arriving point by point, late.
Two confessions where they land: I have slowed time enormously — across a desk the
redefinition arrives in about a nanosecond, and the lateness *is* the wave. And the
needles read the curvature <C k="curv">$F$</C>, while the wave itself rides in
<C k="conn">$A$</C> — the dual-pane mapping the opening figure has been promising.
These compasses are not even an analogy; they read the curvature of the very
connection this section just unfroze.

Each needle's rest direction is that point's local definition of *calm*. And here is
the sentence this article exists to earn, carrying full weight exactly once: a
medium's only essential job is to define calm — the unperturbed state a wave deviates
from; a connection is exactly a definition of "unchanged from point to point," and
nothing more; so the connection is the minimal possible medium — pure comparison, no
substance — and light is a wave in the meaning of "same." (The railway towns rhyme
one last time: news of a time reform running down the telegraph line is a ripple in
the convention itself — though a reform every town adopts at once is pure gauge, the
brush's work, and carries nothing; the ripple light carries is the part no reform can
remove, the curvature.)

$$\frac{\partial^2 A_y}{\partial t^2} \;=\; c^2\,\frac{\partial^2 A_y}{\partial x^2}$$

The waving quantity is $A_y$, the *transverse* component of the plane's pair — the
rotation-per-step for steps taken across the line you are watching. I show you one
line of the plane; the wave is identical along the direction I've hidden. Boundary

Michelson and Morley can now rest. Their instrument hunted the rest frame of a
substance, and a comparison rule is not a substance — no parts, no worldlines,
nothing to be at rest with respect to. The question, not just the answer, was empty;
the aether was less disproved than out-defined. Nothing about the rule is observable
except what survives the brush — its curvature, and its holonomies around loops — and
neither has a rest frame to hunt. The measured isotropy, light's indifference to the
laboratory's own motion, is the rider confessed above: kept, not re-derived.

And the inversion, once, plainly: we never added light to this article. We admitted
that clocks have no shared zero; the ordinary derivative died; the only possible
repair was a compensating field; and the repair, granted the simplest life of its
own, turned out to run at $c$, wave transversally, and carry in-step electric and
magnetic readouts. You don't add light to physics — you can't keep it out. Forces are
what it costs to do calculus in a universe with no absolute conventions, and *light
is a traveling ripple in the rule for comparing phases, existing because calculus had
to be repaired*.

However, the epigraph's boldest clause is still an IOU. It promised a universal
medium for *all* waves — and we hold exactly one, light. The string, sound, the phase
wave the clocks carried: every other wave you have ever met still lives in its own
separate subject, with no bundle in sight.

Found, not made — the coda's crown jewel is a conversation. In 1975, holding the
dictionary, Yang went to Chern and said what any physicist would:

> "This is both thrilling and puzzling, since you mathematicians dreamed up these
> concepts out of nowhere."
>
> "No, no. These concepts were not dreamed up. They were natural and real."
>
> — C. N. Yang and S. S. Chern, 1975

Two communities, no contact, one structure — the twins of 1931 were merely the
sharpest instance. And the epigraph's last clause deserves its honest nuance: none of
this is fringe. It is the standard mathematics of the Standard Model, taught in every
physics graduate program and almost nowhere else — told to the public essentially
once, in 1981, in a magazine article that taught the bundles beautifully and never
let the connection wave. This article is the second telling, with your hands on the
figures this time.

## Further Reading

Jeremy Bernstein and Anthony Phillips, [*Fiber Bundles and Quantum
Theory*](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/fiber-bundles-and-quantum-theory/),
Scientific American, July 1981 (a [free
PDF](https://www.math.stonybrook.edu/~tony/fb-and-qt/Fiber%20Bundles%20and%20Quantum%20Theory.pdf)
survives online). The one popular telling before this one, and a generous teacher
still: it explains the neutron's 720° turn with a Philippine wine dance and curvature
with parallel transport on a rolling cone. What it never does is let the connection
wave — no dynamics, no Maxwell, no light — which is exactly where this article picked
up.

Wu and Yang, *Concept of Nonintegrable Phase Factors and Global Formulation of Gauge
Fields*, Physical Review D, 1975. The dictionary itself — one of the few physics
papers whose centerpiece is a table, and the table is this article's reveal. Reading
it after this lesson gives the strange pleasure of finding your own toys in a museum
case.

John Baez and Javier Muniain, *Gauge Fields, Knots and Gravity*. The gentlest true
book on exactly this ladder — vector fields to connections to curvature to
Yang–Mills, with full honesty and no index gymnastics before you are ready. If this
article left you wanting the real definitions, they are here, in this order.

Tristan Needham, *Visual Differential Geometry and Forms*. Holonomy-first
differential geometry, a kindred spirit in pictures: curvature is defined by carrying
vectors around loops and watching how far they come home turned. Our globe
demonstration is his whole method in miniature.

Richard Feynman, *The Feynman Lectures on Physics*, Volume II, Chapter 15. The
reality of the vector potential argued with characteristic bluntness, years before
Aharonov–Bohm was common knowledge — he weighs what deserves to be called a real
field and concludes that $A$ qualifies. A masterclass in taking bookkeeping seriously
until it confesses to being physics.

Tonomura et al., *Evidence for Aharonov–Bohm Effect with Magnetic Field Completely
Shielded from Electron Wave*, Physical Review Letters, 1986. The experiment of our
reveal, performed with superconducting shielding so that no skeptic could object. The
half-stripe displacement, pinned at exactly half a turn of phase, is one of the
cleanest yes-or-no answers in the history of the subject.

And the seed itself: Eric Weinstein's tweet, quoted at the top. Four sentences that
compress this entire article — the lesson is the long proof that terminates at its
own epigraph.

## Final Words

The next time light crosses a room to reach your eye, there is no jelly quivering in
between. There is a bundle — a circle of phase standing over every point of the
dark — and the light is its geometry ringing: the rule for comparing "same,"
redefining itself point by point, arriving late.

I find it a lasting astonishment that the century that lost its medium had already
been handed a better one, by mathematicians it never thought to call. Understanding
the crossing has not made it less wonderful — and now you own the derivative that
makes it move.
