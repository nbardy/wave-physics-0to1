# Ciechanowski Craft Analysis — 4 Posts, Full Read

Corpus: gears (Feb 2020), internal-combustion-engine (Apr 2021), mechanical-watch (May 2022), bicycle (Mar 2023). ~36,600 words, 310 figures, 276 control mounts total. Publication cadence ≈ 1/year; figure count grows monotonically 30 → 69 → 93 → 118.

## PER POST

### 1. Gears (2020) — 4,890 words, 30 figures, 8 content sections, ~163 words/figure (his densest prose)

**Opening move:** "I've always been fascinated by mechanical gears. There is something captivating about the way their teeth come together to create a fluid, unified motion:" — then an ambient autoplay figure. Hook = **personal confession of fascination**, colon-dropping directly into a hypnotic animation. No promise of utility; the hook is aesthetic.

**Section arc:** Spinning → Disc → Transmission → Torque → Gears → Tangent & Normal → Strings Attached → Multiple Gears. Escalation: define angular velocity (desk fan) → separate angular vs linear velocity (painted dots on a disc) → friction-disc transmission → torque → discs slip, so teeth (title concept arrives in section 5 of 8) → the tooth-shape problem → **payoff = "Strings Attached"**, where the involute is drawn by physically unwinding a string with a marker → composition + the jammed-3-gear anti-pattern as dessert. Everything before "Gears" exists so pitch circles can "reduce their form to the familiar teethless discs."

**Ending move:** "Despite all that, under the murky waters of physical reality, the idealized mathematical principles of gears are still there. I've hopefully shown you that gears aren't just some discs with teeth – there is a lot of mathematical ingenuity in their design." Close = **humility ("hopefully shown you") + reframe of the humble object**, preceded by a Further Watching section recommending others' work (This Old Tony, Brick Experiment Channel, tec-science) with genuine specific praise.

**Figure choreography (2 passages):**
- Figs 15→16 (slip → gears): sentence before fig 15 lists failure conditions ("vibration, wear... manufacturing imprecision, they'll start slipping"); the figure shows the failure; the sentence after names the rescue: "This problem can be solved by ensuring that the driving wheel physically pushes the driven wheel. This is where gears come in." Pattern: *diagnose in prose → witness failure → next figure is the cure*.
- Fig 24 (involute string): before — full physical setup narrated in first person ("We'll take two discs, wrap a string around them... I attached a piece of paper to the underside of the yellow disc so that we can see the trail left by the marker"); after — interpretation of what you just made: "The trail left on the paper is precisely the curve we were looking for." The figure is an *experiment*; prose is protocol before, lab-conclusion after.

**Voice quotes:** "Coincidentally, the triangular pattern of the flattened out arcs is also a simple way to derive the area of a circle." / "in Latin tangere means 'to touch'." / "Even if you never plan to design a functional set of gears, it may be worth knowing how not to design one... Unfortunately the 'three jammed gears' pitfall is quite common in graphic design."

### 2. Internal Combustion Engine (2021) — 6,387 words, 69 figures, 6 content sections, ~93 words/figure

**Opening move:** "The invention of the internal combustion engine in the 19th century has revolutionized transportation over land, water, and air. Despite their omnipresence in modern day, the operation of an engine may be cryptic." Hook = **grand-significance + mystery gap** ("omnipresent yet cryptic"), immediately followed by a draggable full-engine hero.

**Section arc:** Crank → Engine Block → Crankshaft → Pistons → Closing the Lid → Combustion. Two-act structure: Act 1 (Crank) builds a complete toy 4-stroke from first principles via an outrageous stepping stone — **a cannon** ("Thankfully, such device was invented hundreds of years ago – a cannon does exactly what we need") — cannonball fails to crank, becomes a piston. Act 2 rebuilds "a more realistic machine – an engine that one could find in a car," part by part in physical assembly order. **Payoff = Combustion**: pressure/torque/inertia plots culminating in the flywheel, whose job is only comprehensible because you watched torque fluctuate in figs 61–66.

**Ending move:** "Unchallenged for decades, the internal combustion engines in cars are slowly being supplanted by their electric equivalents, which are simpler, quieter, and more environmentally friendly. Despite their drawbacks, classic engines still have something mythical about them – their intricate mechanisms are synchronized together to create carefully controlled conditions for harnessing fire in a truly Promethean way." Close = **elegy for a dying technology + one mythic metaphor** (the most poetic register he permits himself, held for the final clause).

**Figure choreography:**
- Figs 3→4 (cannon → crank-cannon): before fig 3 he installs a visual convention ("The diagonal lines indicate a cross section view – it lets us see what's going on inside an otherwise obscured region"); after fig 4's failure: "We've only been able to do a partial turn of the shaft and the cannon ball is long gone. However, with a few modifications we can harness the pushing power of the explosion in a significantly better way." — failure quantified ("partial turn"), fix promised.
- Figs 41–43 (valve keepers): prose enumerates parts by color ("two {red|valve keepers}, a {yellow|spring retainer}, and an {green|inverted bucket}"), the figure assembles them under a slider, and the after-sentence states the resulting invariant: "Since the keepers are locked in the grooves, the retainer can't move which holds the spring under tension." Assembly figures always end with a *why-it-holds* sentence.

**Voice quotes:** "It's one thing to power something using our own muscles, but the entire point of building an engine is to avoid manual labor and have the device exert the effort instead." / "In our toy engine the valves magically opened and closed on their own, so let's see how it can be actually done in practice." / "there are actually around 50 explosions per second happening in an engine running at that speed."

### 3. Mechanical Watch (2022) — 8,894 words, 93 figures, 11 content sections, ~96 words/figure

**Opening move:** "In the world of modern portable devices, it may be hard to believe that merely a few decades ago the most convenient way to keep track of time was a mechanical watch. Unlike their quartz and smart siblings, mechanical watches can run without using any batteries or other electronic components." Hook = **anachronism-wonder** ("no batteries — how?"), then hero with an x-ray slider "to peek at what's going on inside." He also front-loads a jargon amnesty: "you shouldn't feel pressured to remember them – the names and parts will be color-coded for easy reference."

**Section arc:** Power → Gears → Escapement → Balance → Mainplate → Click → Motion Works → Date → Keyless Works → Automatic Winding → The Size of it All. The first four follow the energy chain (store → transmit → gate → regulate), previewed as "just seven major elements which we can lay out in a straight line" (fig 2). Pivot sentence into Act 2: "we've just been hanging the parts in the air, so it's time we started a proper assembly." Assembly order is dependency order; each section's problem is created by the previous section's solution (the wound spring unwinds itself → Click; hands need setting → Keyless Works). **Payoffs**: fig 27's scrubbed escapement close-up with six sequenced explainer captions (the article's thesis-figure), the Keyless Works ("Personally, I think this entire mechanism known as the keyless works is a real mechanical marvel"), and the emotional finale of "The Size of it All" — a credit-card-scale zoom-out: "if you have one handy you can put it on screen and drag the slider until the card fits in that outline."

**Ending move:** "Despite all these drawbacks, these devices show a true mastery of engineering. With creative use of miniature gears, levers, and springs, a mechanical watch rises from its dormant components to become truly alive." Close = **awe + resurrection metaphor**, after a Further Watching/Reading section (Wristwatch Revival, Daniels' *Watchmaking*).

**Figure choreography:**
- Figs 9→10 (winding): before fig 9 assigns the reader a job ("we're holding the barrel tight, and you can turn the arbor by dragging the slider"); after names what your own experiment revealed: "Notice that as soon as you let go of the arbor... the mainspring will turn the arbor right back. This is less than desired – we want the barrel to turn instead." Fig 10 inverts the constraint. The *reader's hands discover the design requirement*.
- Fig 14 (naive watch): before: "one could naively think that we could just attach a watch hand to the barrel... You can see how this 'watch' behaves after you wind the mainspring"; after: "We clearly have some work to do – the hand spins way too fast and it only does a few rotations..." then arithmetic ("40 × 60 = 2400 complete rotations") manufactures the requirement, and the section ends "This is where gears come in."

**Voice quotes:** "This is where the balance comes in – it forms the beating heart of a watch." / "it's equivalent to giving a push to a person swinging on a swing." / "Here come the three critical pieces of the puzzle." / "There is actually one additional clever bit that's been hiding in plain sight."

### 4. Bicycle (2023) — 16,451 words, 118 figures, 10 content sections, ~139 words/figure

**Opening move:** "There is something delightful about riding a bicycle. Once mastered, the simple action of pedaling to move forward and turning the handlebars to steer makes bike riding an effortless activity." Hook = **shared embodied delight** ("There is something delightful/captivating about..." — same construction as gears), then a steerable rider hero. Thesis stated as inversion: "most of their parts operate in plain sight. What's not directly visible are all the forces."

**Section arc:** Forces → Moments → Up and Down → Forwards and Backwards → Left and Right → Stability → Wheels → Simple Frame → Bending → Stresses in the Frame. Three acts: (1) Newton primer with wooden boxes (2 sections, 15 figures before a bicycle appears in earnest); (2) rigid-body bike decomposed by axis — with a deliberate scare figure (fig 17, the full force-arrow soup: "it's easy to get overwhelmed with all the arrows pointing in different directions. We'll eventually understand all of these forces") — **payoff #1 = countersteering** ("this is exactly how a bicycle turns!"); Stability then *deconstructs* two popular explanations; (3) internal forces — **payoff #2 = spokes-work-in-tension reveal** (figs 74–90), **payoff #3 = deriving the diamond frame from FEM stress maps** (fig 118: "We've more or less arrived at the modern bicycle frame").

**Ending move:** "The interactions of a single particle in the ground, a tire, or a frame, are completely insignificant, but once we accumulate the effects of all the molecules in these bodies, they end up forming a visible manifestation that makes bicycles rideable. Perhaps the next time you're on a bicycle, you'll be able to conceptualize some of those invisible forces that make the ride so enjoyable." Close = **zoom-out to atoms + a benediction sending the reader back into the world**. Further Reading here is scholarly (two PhD theses, the canonical stability paper, Ruina's lecture).

**Figure choreography:**
- Figs 51→52 (countersteer): before fig 51 plants the trap: "One could assume that to start turning left from a vertical position, the rider also has to turn the handlebars to the left. In the demonstration below, you can see what happens in that scenario:"; after: "Notice that tires generate forces that lean the bicycle to the right. However, to turn left we need to lean to the left as well." Fig 52 shows the real move; after: "This may sound quite surprising, but this is exactly how a bicycle turns!" — hypothesis → simulated refutation → reveal → one of only two exclamation marks in 36k words.
- Figs 74→76 (loose spokes): before poses an experiment on a deliberately half-built artifact ("Let's see what happens if we now put a vertical load on this wheel with nipples just loosely tightened"); after diagnoses in color-anchored prose ("Those bottom spokes are simply dangling from the hub without supporting any of the load"), and pivots on an observation of the failed figure itself: "Notice, however, that the large number of spokes doesn't participate in the load bearing at all. If we could make sure that these spokes are working too..." — the fix is *motivated by pointing at the failure demo*.

**Voice quotes:** "Let's introduce the unassuming protagonist of this section – a plain wooden box." / "Even though we leave the green pastures behind and enter the kingdom of infinite checkerboard pavement, we at least make it easier to show these forces from different angles." / "one can't make oneself heavier by just pressing harder on the ground."

## CROSS-POST SYNTHESIS

### VOICE FINGERPRINT (quote-backed)

1. **Long, even, comma-chained sentences.** Mean 26–29 words/sentence in every post; median 22–26; 12–18% exceed 40 words; under 5% are shorter than 10 words. There are no punchy fragments, no one-sentence paragraphs for drama. The rhythm is a steady legato.
2. **Zero rhetorical questions.** 0 question marks in ~36,600 words across all four posts. Wondering is always declarativized: "You may wonder why we need this complicated mechanism in the first place." / "It may be then a little puzzling why a typical frame connects these four locations with two triangular patterns."
3. **Two exclamation marks total** (both in bicycle, both at genuine physics surprises: "the wheel ends up rotating around the blue axis!" and "this is exactly how a bicycle turns!"). Exclamation is a rationed resource spent only on counterintuitive truth.
4. **Three-persona pronoun system.** "We" = builder-team (watch: 145 we + 17 we'll + 14 we've; bicycle: 212/21/37): "we'll assemble the keyless works." "You" = experimenter at the controls ("you can" — 9–67×/post: "you can witness," "you can tweak," "you can scrub back and forth in time"). "I" = stagehand who rigs the scene (24–35×/post): "I temporarily removed the screw to make things easier to see"; "I put a little black dot on that gear"; "I'll hide the rest of the engine." First-person appears almost exclusively to confess a visualization intervention.
5. **"Let's" is the drivetrain of the prose** — 14 (gears) / 22 (ICE) / 35 (watch) / 59 (bicycle) uses; nearly every section and figure setup begins "Let's look at / Let's see / Let's explore."
6. **Canonical figure-naming formula**: "In the demonstration below" (13/6/18/42 per post, 79 total), with variants "in the simulation below." Figures are never numbered or referenced backwards by number; callbacks re-summon the object instead ("Let me bring up the relaxed spring one more time").
7. **Attention is verbally directed, discovery is credited to the reader.** "Notice that" (70 total), "Observe," "Recall that" (13), and — his most flattering move — "You may have noticed / You may have realized / You've probably experienced this yourself," which retroactively attributes his observation to you: "You may also have noticed a subtle dance between the little horn... and the notched disk."
8. **Jargon protocol: mechanism first, name second.** The term always trails the demonstrated behavior, attached by "known as / so-called / called": "in an action known as hacking"; "joined in the parameter s known as the slip ratio"; "a so-called pitch circle"; "it's known as a cam." Plus etymology as reward: the click "makes a clicking sound, which explains its name"; "in Latin tangere means 'to touch'"; keyless "explains the keyless name."
9. **Emotion is carried by discourse markers, not adjectives**: "Unfortunately," (16×) always introduces the failure of the naive design; "Thankfully," (6×) introduces the engineered rescue. E.g., "Unfortunately, that won't really work... Thankfully, the designers of this watch movement used an ingenious way..."
10. **Praise flows to artifacts and their designers, never to himself**: "ingenious," "clever bit," "The brilliant solution that bicycle wheel designers came up with," "a real mechanical marvel." The only self-referential evaluation is hedged: "I've hopefully shown you."
11. **Parts are animate; the reader never is.** Springs "want" ("this spring is quite strong and it wants to expand very rapidly"), mainsprings scheme ("the mainspring finds an easy way to release its tension"), forces are "eager" ("the braking force will eagerly try to rotate the bike"), the escape wheel does jail time ("we let the escape wheel briefly escape from that jail"). But he never says "you might be confused" — reader-competence is axiomatic.
12. **Diminutive affection**: "little" 22×/42× in watch/bicycle — "little horn," "little lever," "little black dot," "little time keeping mechanism." Paired with hedges "slightly, roughly, a little, more or less, gently" that keep claims calibrated.
13. **Household-experiment asides** ground every abstraction: school ruler (bending), uncooked spaghetti (tension vs buckling), empty aluminum can (thin-wall buckling), door hinge (torque), person on a swing (resonance), credit card (scale), tug of war (pretension).
14. **Present tense, always.** The machine perpetually operates now; past tense reserved for history sentences (quartz in the 1970s, the 19th century, "vehicles built in a bygone era").
15. **Reused boilerplate**: the animation-pause paragraph appears nearly verbatim in all four ("By default all animations are enabled, but if you find them distracting, or if you want to save power, you can globally pause all animations"), as do hero instructions ("You can drag the device around to change your viewing angle") and integrity disclaimers ("what you see here is a simplified visualization"; "Note that the size of the gap in the demonstration is not to scale").
16. **What he NEVER does**: numbered figure references; captions; questions; jokes at the reader's expense; pop-culture references; motivational filler ("this is really important!"); apologies for difficulty; second-person imperatives ("click the slider" → always the permissive "you can drag"); unresolved cliffhangers — every planted element pays off.

### DESIGN GRAMMAR

- **One knob per figure is the law.** Control-count distribution: watch {0:10, 1:81, 2:1, 3:1}; ICE {0:36, 1:33}; gears {0:5, 1:24, 2:1}; bicycle {0:10, 1:86, 2:21, 3:1}. Across 310 figures: 78% have ≤1 control; max is 3 (twice, ever). Two-slider figures are reserved for genuine 2-parameter spaces (spring stiffness × moment of inertia; linear × angular velocity for slip; hub width × side force).
- **Control-type ratio**: ~97% sliders (ids `_sl0/_sl1`), ~1% segmented buttons (3 total: gear-teeth ratio picker, pallet-fork left/right buttons, beam-material picker), rest ambient. Autoplay (zero-control) share varies hugely: ICE 52%, gears 17%, watch 11%, bicycle 8% — ICE is a film you watch; watch and bicycle are instruments you play. Crucially, many sliders are **time scrubbers** ("The slider controls the progress of time"; "you can scrub back and forth in time to see all the action as it happens") — he converts animation into reader-paced, reversible replay, which is the real function of "interactivity" here.
- **Persistent protagonist**: one artifact per article, built cumulatively and never swapped out. The watch movement accretes across 93 figures with stable color identity (barrel = red, arbor = yellow, mainspring = gray, jewels = pink) from fig 6 to fig 93. ICE assembles one inline-four. Bicycle keeps one rider+bike, plus section-level protagonists explicitly introduced as characters ("the unassuming protagonist of this section – a plain wooden box"). Sub-assemblies get their own POV shots and then rejoin the whole ("Let's attach the entire balance assembly to the rest of the movement").
- **Colored vocabulary is the legend — there are no labels in the figures.** Span densities: gears 7 (pre-system, mostly formatting), ICE 199, watch 467 (~1 per 19 words), bicycle 510. The innovation clearly arrives with ICE (2021). Color even encodes *actions*, not just parts: `{color_turn|turn the crown}`, `{color_pull|pull the crown}`, and prose points at annotations ("I'm pointing its location with a gray arrow"). Rendering of the prose and the canvas are one system; the sentence physically matches the pixels.
- **Camera grammar**, always verbally announced: cross-section ("The diagonal lines indicate a cross section view"), hiding ("To explain how this mechanism works I'll first hide all the unrelated parts"), cutting holes ("I also cut a hole in the top part of the barrel so that you can see the spring inside"), transparency ("I made the rim semitransparent"), slow motion ("from up close and in slow motion"), and the terminal zoom-out (credit card). Aspect-ratio metadata (bicycle/watch) shows deliberate sizing: ratio_20–40 for flat physics diagrams, ratio_100 for 3D scenes.
- **Figure cadence**: one figure every 93–163 words; never more than ~3 paragraphs of unillustrated prose anywhere in 36k words.

### PEDAGOGY PATTERNS

1. **Broken version first / falsify the naive hypothesis.** Marker phrase "One could (naively) assume/think..." appears in all four posts. Watch: hand glued to barrel spins wildly (fig 14); 343:1 single gear pair ("As you can see, these proportions are ridiculous"). ICE: cannonball vs crank (fig 4). Gears: slipping friction discs (fig 15); jammed three gears (fig 30). Bicycle: steer-left-to-go-left (fig 51); loose-nipple wheel (fig 74); locked-handlebar gyroscope myth (fig 53). The failure is always *simulated, not described*.
2. **Simplify, confess the magic, then un-simplify.** "In our toy engine the valves magically opened and closed on their own, so let's see how it can be actually done in practice." / "In the magical world of these simulations I removed all friction or air resistance." Coil spring → torsion spring → real S-shaped mainspring; friction discs → involute teeth; toy cylinder → real head with valve timing.
3. **Arithmetic creates the problem the next mechanism solves.** "the second hand should cover around 40 × 60 = 2400 complete rotations" → gear train; "8 beats per second or 28,800 beats per hour"; "around 50 explosions per second"; "the ratio of teeth... to be around 343:1" → multi-stage reduction.
4. **Chekhov's lever.** Parts are planted with an IOU and redeemed sections later: "we're also going to attach a little lever that we will get back to at a later point" (watch fig 43) → "There is actually one additional clever bit that's been hiding in plain sight. If you recall, we put a small lever right on the mainplate..." (fig 80). "Recall that..." (13×) is the standard callback operator.
5. **Scare-then-decompose.** Show the full complexity once, early, to justify the slow path: bicycle fig 17's arrow soup ("it's easy to get overwhelmed... we'd preferably start with a more welcoming approach"); watch hero x-ray followed by "just seven major elements which we can lay out in a straight line."
6. **Section-final savior sentence.** Nearly every section ends by naming the next component as the answer to a freshly created problem: "This is where gears come in." / "we'll do this with the escapement." / "This is where the balance comes in – it forms the beating heart of a watch." / "This is where the yoke comes in." The whole article is a chain of engineered cliff-resolutions.
7. **Honest simplification disclosures** at load-bearing moments: "Note that the flame propagation inside a cylinder is quite complex, and what you see here is a simplified visualization"; "I need to emphasize that we've only conducted a very simplified investigation of a bicycle frame"; "what keeps bicycles balanced... is still an active area of research."
8. **Homework in the physical world**: "The next time you tighten a nut with a wrench try to see how the force needed..."; "you can easily test this by trying to bend an empty aluminum can"; the on-screen credit card. The final bicycle sentence generalizes the move: "Perhaps the next time you're on a bicycle..."

### MATH HANDLING

- **Equation density collapses over time.** Gears (2020) has ~20 display-equation slots, chained derivation style: "The linear velocity v_in as seen by the input disc is: ... Since these velocities are the same we can simply equate them: ... To finally derive that..." ICE has ~4 (displacement V = A·S·n; T = Iα presented as "a rotational equivalent of traditional linear equation" F = ma). Bicycle has ~5 with an explicit policy statement: "While in this article I'll mostly avoid the underlying mathematical details, the relation tying the applied force F, the mass m, and the output acceleration a... deserves to be presented in one of the most fundamental equations in physics" — F=ma is granted a ceremony. **Watch has zero display equations**; only inline arithmetic (40 × 60 = 2400).
- **Equations never lead.** The phenomenon is always played with first; the equation arrives as a summary receipt, variables pre-named in prose ("angular velocity denoted with a Greek letter omega ω"). Units are actively de-emphasized: "I'll often skip the units completely since they won't be essential."
- **Substitutes for equations:** (a) *physical constructions* — the involute drawn by an actual string and marker; flattened arc-traces to compare path lengths; moment balance shown as equal-area colored rectangles ("drawing colored rectangles spanning the length of the acting force arrow and the force arm"); (b) *plots-with-a-slider* — Pacejka tire curves under a vertical-load slider, cam-profile lift plot, torque/inertia plots (ICE figs 58–66); (c) *proportional reasoning in prose* — "the moment that a slice exerts grows with a square of that distance"; (d) *outsourcing rigor* — "if you're interested you can follow a more rigorous derivation that I found."

### CRITIQUE — costs of the method

1. **Enormous commitment, no skim path.** Bicycle is ~16.5k words / 118 figures (hours with interaction); there are no summaries, no TOC in the body, no recaps at section boundaries, and callbacks assume full retention ("Recall that the rod bearings are lubricated by the oil coming in through crankshaft passages" — from 30 figures earlier). A reader who bails gets nothing consolidated. Likeliest bail points: gears' Tangent & Normal derivation run (figs 19–23, five abstract figures plus equations); watch's Keyless Works (22 figures of interacting levers named yoke / setting lever / corrector lever / setting lever jumper — peak working-memory load); bicycle's Bending chapter (a materials-mechanics course inserted at word ~13,000).
2. **Metronomic pacing.** Every beat is setup-sentence → figure → "Notice that..." for 30–118 cycles; words-per-figure barely varies. No people, no history beyond a sentence, no register changes. The watch effectively teaches the escapement twice (floating, then assembled) and the wind-blocking problem three times before the click resolves it — thoroughness at the price of monotony for fast readers.
3. **Accessibility is structurally sacrificed.** Meaning lives in color-coded words (467–510 spans/post) bound to unlabeled, caption-less canvases. Colorblind users must distinguish red/green/orange/yellow part names; screen-reader, print, and low-power readers lose the primary channel entirely — sentences like "The {color_cyan|teal components} can freely slide on the {color_gray|hairspring}" or "You may have noticed a subtle dance between the little horn... and the notched disk" are unrecoverable without the rendered figure. The prose deliberately does *not* stand alone.
4. **Interaction is mostly confirmation, not experiment.** ~97% of controls are single sliders, and a large fraction are time scrubbers; the reader replays the author's simulation rather than testing their own prediction. The "one could assume..." traps are sprung *by the author* — the reader is never asked to commit to a guess before the reveal. Genuine multi-DOF exploration exists in maybe a dozen figures out of 310. Relatedly, the grammar is nearly non-transferable: each post requires bespoke 3D simulation engineering (hence ~1 post/year) — this is a craft that doesn't scale and can't be imitated cheaply.
5. **Intuition without numeracy.** Outside a few pet numbers, quantities are unitless arrows and brightness maps ("The brighter the element the higher the stress"). After 16k words on bicycles you cannot estimate spoke pretension in newtons, brake force, or frame stress margins; gears explicitly waives units. The posts can produce confident readers who cannot compute anything — understanding-shaped feeling rather than operational knowledge.
6. **Negative results are asserted, not demonstrated.** In the stability section, the decisive counterexamples (counter-rotating-wheel bicycle, zero-trail-zero-gyro self-stable bicycle) — the evidence that demolishes the popular explanations — appear only as single prose sentences, while the seductive-but-insufficient gyroscope/trail mechanisms get ~8 interactive figures. The demo-weighting is inversely proportional to epistemic importance, and a skimming reader will exit believing exactly the myths the text disclaims.
7. **Jargon amnesty is partially false.** Watch says "you shouldn't feel pressured to remember them," then fig 86's prose requires simultaneous retention of ratchet wheel (fig 44), barrel (fig 6), mainspring (fig 5), and click (fig 46): "the {orange|last one} connecting to the {green|ratchet wheel} that is used to wind the mainspring hidden inside the {red|barrel}." Color mitigates but does not remove the ~40-part vocabulary load; comprehension degrades quietly for anyone reading in one sitting.

### Distilled formula (one paragraph)
Open with wonder about a familiar object + immediately playable hero; declare the color-code contract; build ONE persistent artifact from first principles, where each section ends by manufacturing a problem ("Unfortunately...") that the next component solves ("This is where X comes in"); demonstrate every failure mode as a simulation before showing the fix; one slider per figure, prose-as-legend via color-matched words; "we" builds, "you" experiments, "I" rigs the camera; quantify only to motivate (N rotations/sec), never to compute; close with Further Reading that praises others, then a two-sentence elegiac zoom-out that hands the object back to the reader's daily life.
