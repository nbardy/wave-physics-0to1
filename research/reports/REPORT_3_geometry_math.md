## MATH-MADE-TOUCHABLE (curves-and-surfaces + tesseract deep dive)

### How equations enter in curves-and-surfaces (the only heavily-equation post of the six)
The invariant sequence is: **interactive → observed property → plain-word statement → symbol substitution announced as a compression → formula → boundary-value sanity check → formula re-rendered as another interactive.** Concretely:

1. **The lerp equation.** Preceded by Fig 8 (weight plots under a slider: "As we progress through the curve using the slider we decrease the weight of the point A and increase the weight of the point B"). Then symbols are introduced as an apology for word length: *"We can describe those little plots using some equations. A word 'progress' would make the formulas very long so I'll replace it with the traditionally used letter t."* Then he invents **glyph typography for terms**: *"We'll see those (1 − t) and t terms a lot, so to make them easier to distinguish I'll mark them and I'll put little triangles on their right sides to quickly visualize if that section goes up or down with the progress – those triangles match the shapes of the plots we've just seen."* Then verification: *"We can indeed verify that e.g. plugging progress t equal to 1 simply returns the point B."*
2. **Operational semantics before algebra.** He refuses to let notation carry undefined meaning: *"It may not be immediately clear what it means to multiply a point by a number so let's explore that a little"* → Fig 9/10 animate x,y coordinates scaling under the slider.
3. **The bilinear patch formula** is sandwiched: relabeled diagram first ("Let me show the initial demonstration with different points labeled to makes things easier to reference"), equations, then instant defusal: *"The terms next to the control points may seem a little complicated, but in fact they're fairly simple once represented visually"* → Fig 20 renders each coefficient as a draggable red "weight hat" surface. **Every coefficient of every formula eventually gets its own interactive plot.**
4. **Derivation as drama.** The quadratic Bézier is *discovered*, not defined: lock two sliders (Fig 22), then *"Let's try to write the equation of that curve"*, then a geometric move replaces an algebraic one — he overlaps points B and C in an interactive (Fig 23) to collapse the formula: "Observe that this makes the entire surface flat and the curve gets nicely isolated." De Casteljau emerges from rearrangement narrated with affect: *"It may seem that we only made things worse, but in fact we simplified things greatly. Notice that the values in the parentheses are just linear interpolations between points A and B."*
5. **Notational refusal at scale.** *"The equation driving that surface is so massive that I'll just represent the [s, 1−s, t, 1−t] parts using their triangular symbols."* And Catmull-Clark rules are given as a colored bullet list, not formulas: *"The arithmetical procedures needed to be done aren't particularly important here."* Same move in GPS: *"for the rest of the article I'll keep using the more abstract description of the problem since the mathematical operations aren't critical for our understanding of GPS."* He budgets formality: symbols appear only where they'll be *reused*.
6. **Physical formulas get physical payoffs**: κ = 1/R is immediately re-grounded (*"Note that the inverse relation makes sense – a small curvature implies a not very curvy region"*), and F = mV²/R is cashed out as *"a very unpleasant jerk"* felt by train passengers — curvature continuity literally made touchable.

### Tesseract: zero equations
The most abstract post contains **no formula blocks at all**. Math is made touchable through (a) *operations* (stretch, project, rotate, slice — each is a slider), and (b) *counting as proof*: "It's bounded by 6 squares, which are bounded by 12 line segments, which in turn are bounded by 8 corners"; "There are 16 vertices total and each vertex belongs to 4 edges and 6 faces." The hypercube table replaces any combinatorial formula.

### Dimensional-analogy strategy (2D→3D→4D)
1. **One operation, induced from trivial cases:** point→segment→square→cube each via "take what we had and stretch it into the new dimension," so 4D is a *fourth application of a rehearsed verb*: "The procedure to create the fourth dimension is no different."
2. **Pre-install the error theory in the familiar dimension.** Before showing anything 4D, a full section demonstrates that 2D drawings of 3D are already ambiguous and angle-distorting ("In the simulation they're 120° apart, but we know that those angles are equal to 90°"). So when the tesseract projection looks wrong, the reader has a category for it: "we've already seen how a projection can skew squares into trapezoids and a similar thing happens here with the cubes."
3. **Flag the one unverifiable step:** *"This next sentence requires a leap of faith. Just like a projection of a 3D object onto a 2D space creates a 2D object, a projection of a 4D object onto a 3D space forms a 3D object."* One leap, explicitly licensed; everything else is analogy-checked.
4. **Every 4D demo has a 3D→2D twin placed adjacently:** cube-shadow (Fig 17) before tesseract-shadow (Fig 18); morphing yellow face in 2D shadow (Fig 27) to explain the morphing highlighted cube in 3D projection (Fig 26); cube-through-plane slices (Fig 28) before tesseract-through-space slices (Fig 29). Doctrine stated outright: *"don't be afraid to fallback to a 3D cube in the search for explanations of the tesseract's unusual nature."*
5. **Choose the abstraction that generalizes:** axis-of-rotation is replaced by plane-of-rotation *because* it survives the dimension jump — "a plane of rotation is a more universal way of describing rotations since it works in 2D, 3D, 4D, and higher dimensions... in four dimensions we can't even define an axis of rotation."
6. **Honesty about the ceiling:** "Our human brains are hardwired to understand things in three dimensions – reasoning about the fourth one requires some amount of imagination or even delusion... We can try to understand how things work in 4D, but I don't think we can fully experience it."

### Coordinates / vectors / interpolation notation
- Points get letter names (A, B, C, D) and get **renamed when the cast changes**: "Note that we're dealing with just three points so from now on I'll call the third point C instead of D."
- Parameters are sliders before they are letters; the (s,t) parameter pair becomes a **literal draggable 1×1 square widget** ("we can think of the choice they offer as a 1×1 square") that then recurs as a mini-map inset "in the bottom left corner of a demonstration" — parameter space itself is a persistent UI object.
- Axes and planes are colored chips inline in prose ({x}, {w}, {xy}, {zw}); GPS typesets the unknowns of its equation system as color-highlighted tokens ({x}{y}{z}, bias {b}) matching figure colors — **variables are color-bound to their pixels**.
- Vectors are always named arrows: "The {blue arrow} shows the surface normal... The {yellow arrow} points towards the light source" — never bare boldface symbols.

### DESIGN GRAMMAR
- **Ring structure with a hero figure.** All six open with Figure 1 = the finished artifact (constellation, camera, lit scene, mask, slicing tesseract, illuminated globe) plus an explicit IOU: "By the end of this article the effects of light in this demonstration should become more clear." Curves and tesseract literally re-mount the hero as the final figure (Fig 90 = cs_subdiv_hero2; tesseract Fig 29 ≈ Fig 1 with controls exposed). The article is a proof that terminates at its own epigraph.
- **Colored vocabulary is the load-bearing referencing system.** 473 spans / 92 classes. Prose words are painted the color of the object they name ({figurine|yellow figurine}, {bias|bias}, {user_clock|our clock} vs {system_clock|system clock}), eliminating all "see the dashed line in figure 3" deixis — the word *is* the pointer. Class inventories mirror conceptual load: GPS's clock-bias argument needs 38 classes; lights-and-shadows needs only 3 arrows.
- **Knob budget escalates with reader competence.** Early figures: 0–1 slider. GPS's clock demos grow 1→2 sliders as bias enters; curves' late figures carry 3; tesseract's finale carries 4 and is flagged ("Behold the sliders galore!"). Ratios: curves 1.12 controls/figure, gps 0.87, tesseract 0.52, cameras 0.32 (cameras substitutes in-canvas dragging). New controls are always introduced one at a time and named by color in prose ("the {black slider} controls the aperture, the {blue slider} controls the focal length").
- **Persistent protagonist objects** amortize orientation cost: GPS's yellow figurine + three colored landmarks + right-hand map survive ~26 figures; cameras photographs one red-sphere/green-sphere scene for 56 figures; curves carries the red pen, the input-square inset, and control-point dots throughout; earth-and-sun's red subsolar dot recurs across reference frames. Once a visual noun is taught it is never redesigned.
- **2D vs 3D selection rule: 3D for phenomena, 2D for measurement.** He drops dimensions whenever a value must be *read*: "To make things easier to see let's briefly drop down to a two dimensional case" (GPS accuracy); "We can see this situation even better from a side view" (lights cosine); tesseract's entire method is dimension-dropping. The ratio metadata encodes it: curves' 2D plots are ratio_50, 3D scenes ratio_100. Conversely 3D appears only when the phenomenon *is* spatial (spheres intersecting, orbital planes, patches).
- **Dual-pane simultaneity:** world+map (GPS), scene+photo (cameras), 3D-object+projection (tesseract), top-down+observer-view (earth, lights car demo), curve+weight-plot (curves). One canvas shows reality, the twin shows the representation, and the interaction drives both — the mapping *between* them is the lesson.
- **Plots as unwrapped physical objects:** "a timeline that also tracks the duration of flight using a bit more compact representation – you can think of it as a clock unwrapped from an arc shape to a straight line." Abstract chart axes are always derived on-screen from a concrete predecessor.

### PEDAGOGY PATTERNS
1. **Build the wrong thing first.** Spiral-knob UI ("it's impossible to create an S-shaped curve using these controls"), active sound beacons ("fatal flaw"), bare sensor ("doesn't really resemble anything"), geostationary constellation ("we wouldn't be able to tell if we're on southern or northern latitudes"), janky parabola basis, naive 365-day calendar. The failure specifies the requirements for the next invention.
2. **Constraint-relaxation ladder:** tapes→drones→sound→radio; 1 landmark→2→3→4; 2D→3D; ideal clock→biased clock; noiseless→noisy. One assumption removed per section, monotonically approaching the real system.
3. **Boundary-value sanity checks after every formalism:** "plugging progress t equal to 1 simply returns the point B"; "When eccentricity is 0, an ellipse becomes a perfect circle"; "When an angle measures 1 radian, the length of the yellow arc is equal to the length of the blue radius"; "On the straight part the radius of curvature is infinite so the force is 0."
4. **Awe-numbers deployed only after intuition is built:** "31,508,400 wave cycles per bit, which, at 50 bits per second, ends up as the exact 1575.42 MHz"; "the radius of the Sun is 109 times larger"; "It takes about 25,772 years." Numbers are dessert, never vegetables.
5. **Everyday anchors:** lightning-to-thunder counting, squinting-as-aperture ("Your eyelids covering some part of your iris simply act as an aperture"), movie bokeh homework ("Next time you watch a movie pay a close attention to the shape of out-of-focus highlights"), train jerk, overcast-sky ambient light, Polaris ceasing to be the north star.
6. **One-delta figures:** consecutive demos differ by exactly one element — GPS spheres 1→2→3 (Figs 21–23), bounce counts 0→1→2→3, hypercube dimensions. The diff *is* the concept.
7. **Enumerated harvest after rich demos:** "There are many interesting observations to be done here:" followed by 3–4 bullets (tesseract shadow, cameras cosine-fourth factors, curves quadratic-weights) — he decompresses his own dense figures.
8. **Recap-before-reuse across and within posts:** "I've discussed ellipses on this blog before, but it's worth doing a quick recap"; "If you recall our discussion of sharpness..."; "Recall that each subframe of a GPS message is timestamped with a granularity of 6 seconds."
9. **Forward-promissory notes to sustain tension:** "that concept deserves a bit more elaboration"; "we'll have to wait until the later parts of this article"; "those details are fascinating – I'll discuss them in the last two sections."
10. **Reader-preference accommodations as pedagogy:** metric/imperial toggles woven into sentences, global animation pause "if you want to save power," and honesty about arbitrariness ("sharpness is ultimately arbitrary – it depends on the size at which the final image is seen").

1. **Stamina cliffs in the long posts.** GPS is ~9.5k words / 62 figures / 16 sections with *two* payoff peaks; a reader satisfied by the trilateration climax at Fig 18 ("a monumental achievement") still faces 44 figures, and the final signal-processing third (PRN codes, correlation) is effectively a second article grafted on. There is no table of contents, no skimming affordance, no "if you only read one section" routing.
2. **Figure-density droughts mark the bail points.** Earth-and-sun averages 177 words/figure and its Year section (tropical year, Julian/Gregorian leap arithmetic) is nearly demo-free prose; GPS's Navigation Message section describes a 25-frame × 5-subframe × 10-word × 30-bit hierarchy across ~600 words with **zero** figures — the most diagrammable data structure in the corpus gets none. When his own medium disappears, the writing alone is merely competent.
3. **Formula-avoidance caps out below implementation depth.** GPS punts the 4-equation pseudorange solve ("the mathematical operations aren't critical"); curves never writes the Bernstein basis, the B-spline recurrence, or Catmull-Clark weights explicitly ("The arithmetical procedures... aren't particularly important here"). A reader who wants to *build* a NURBS evaluator or a GPS solver leaves without the actual recurrences — touchability is achieved partly by amputating the last 20% of rigor, and the Further Reading list is the silent apology.
4. **Color is a single point of failure.** The core device — prose spans color-bound to figure elements ({landmark_red} vs {landmark_green}, red vs blue clock bars, six axis-plane hues in tesseract) — is invisible to colorblind readers and meaningless in print/screen-reader contexts; the interactives are mandatory, not supplementary ("dragging around to change the viewing angle will do wonders for understanding what's going on"). There is no static-fallback narrative path.
5. **Mid-post prerequisite detours are where momentum dies.** Cameras interrupts its camera-building at the exact midpoint for a Waves section (phase velocity, index of refraction, Fresnel equations) — the reader who came for bokeh hits Snell's law; lights-and-shadows pauses shadows for a two-section math toolkit (Radians, Solid Angles) hung on a car demo. The detours are well-made but structurally announce "eat your vegetables," and neither post signals how long the detour will last.
6. **Late-post cognitive load outruns the scaffolding.** Curves' non-uniform knot figures (71–73) require holding "order-k functions built over shifting knot intervals" from one paragraph while operating two sliders; tesseract's finale stacks four sliders on a projection the author himself calls trippy. He acknowledges the difficulty ("I personally find it quite difficult to see what's going on") without adding remediation — the acknowledgment substitutes for the fix.
7. **The endings are a template.** Six posts, one closing waltz: Further Reading → Final Words → "I find it fascinating/captivating/inspiring that..." → humanistic aphorism. Read in sequence, the aphorisms ("painting with light," "beautiful curves and surfaces," "forever be tied to Earth and Sun") become interchangeable and lose force; the craft everywhere else is bespoke, the landings are stamped.
8. **He anticipates delight but not confusion.** The "you may have noticed" apparatus models a reader who explores successfully; there are no self-check questions, no common-misconception callouts beyond the ones he pre-planned to debunk (GPS relativity), and no guidance when a reader's slider experiment produces something the prose didn't predict.
