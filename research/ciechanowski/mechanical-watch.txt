# Mechanical Watch


In the world of modern portable devices, it may be hard to believe that merely a few decades ago the most convenient way to keep track of time was a mechanical watch. Unlike their quartz and smart siblings, mechanical watches can run without using any batteries or other electronic components.

Over the course of this article I’ll explain the workings of the mechanism seen in the demonstration below. You can drag the device around to change your viewing angle, and you can use the slider to peek at what’s going on inside:


[FIGURE 1 | hero | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | hero_sl0]

What you see here is known as the movement – the inner part of a mechanical watch that’s usually enclosed in a metal case. In this article I’m focusing on a watch movement itself, since beautiful watch cases merely hide the intricate mechanisms which are the real stars of the show.

The entire watch movement has a lot of parts, and in this blog post I’ll explain the purpose of each one. The world of watchmaking is jargon-heavy, so many of the components may have unfamiliar names, but you shouldn’t feel pressured to remember them – the names and parts will be {color_red|color-coded} for easy reference.

In a functioning watch many parts are in constant motion. It may not look like much, but these parts still have a lot of interesting details about them that contribute to the {color_hand|second hand} rotating at a correct pace. We’ll start exploring these details by focusing on the source of power for this entire contraption.


# Power


Purely mechanical devices have a few different ways to power themselves, but one of the simplest methods to store energy is to use a spring. Most springs we see in daily life are coil springs. In the demonstration below, you can move the {color_red|mass} attached to this type of {color_yellow|spring} to see it bounce:


[FIGURE 3 | coil_spring | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | coil_spring_sl0]

When a {color_yellow|spring} like this is compressed, it stores some energy that is then released when the compressing tension is removed. Mechanical watches typically use a different kind of spring – a spiral torsion spring. This type of {color_yellow|spring} is loaded when it’s twisted. When let go, the {color_yellow|spring} unwinds in the opposite direction to eventually settle in its natural state:


[FIGURE 4 | torsion_spring | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | torsion_spring_sl0]

In a mechanical watch, we ultimately want to show rotating hands, so a spinning motion that a torsion spring provides is particularly useful. A {color_gray|spring} in a typical mechanical watch has a slightly more complicated shape – you can see it below in its relaxed state. By dragging the {color_gray|slider} you can try to wind it midair, but as soon as you let go, it will snap back to its original shape:


[FIGURE 5 | mainspring_s | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | mainspring_s_sl0]

As you can see, this {color_gray|spring} is quite strong and it wants to expand very rapidly. To contain the {color_gray|spring} we have to put it in a casing known as a {color_red|barrel}:


[FIGURE 6 | mainspring_barrel | ratio_100]

Once in the {color_red|barrel}, the {color_gray|spring} still wants to expand to its original state, but the {color_red|barrel’s} wall keep it in place. This {color_gray|spring} is the storage of energy for the watch and its name, the mainspring, reflects its importance.

Unfortunately, we can’t really get any useful work from the {color_gray|mainspring} in this state – it has already expanded to the largest possible size. To store more energy in it we need to wind it tightly using the {color_yellow|arbor} that we’ll first attach on the inner side of the {color_gray|mainspring}:

If you look closely, the {color_gray|mainspring} has a little hole near its end – you can see it in the center of the demonstration. The {color_yellow|arbor} has a little hook that grabs onto that hole:

When the {color_yellow|arbor} is turned, it pulls the {color_gray|mainspring} with it, causing it to wind. In the demonstration below, we’re holding the {color_red|barrel} tight, and you can turn the {color_yellow|arbor} by dragging the slider:

Notice that as soon as you let go of the {color_yellow|arbor} by releasing the slider, the {color_gray|mainspring} will turn the {color_yellow|arbor} right back. This is less than desired – we want the {color_red|barrel} to turn instead, so that it can power the other parts of the watch. To get some useful work from the {color_gray|mainspring}, we’ll have to keep holding on to the {color_yellow|arbor} and instead let the {color_red|barrel} go when we want to use the stored energy:


[FIGURE 10 | mainspring_wind1 | ratio_100]

We’ll soon see how this is accomplished in practice, but for now we’ll assume that the {color_yellow|arbor} is held tight and the {color_gray|mainspring} ends up rotating the {color_red|barrel}, just like in the demonstration above. Before we finish up with the {color_gray|mainspring} and the {color_red|barrel}, let’s discuss two other details that make this mechanism more reliable. Let me bring up the relaxed spring one more time:


[FIGURE 11 | mainspring_s2 | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | mainspring_s2_sl0]

The {color_blue|metal strip} attached to the {color_gray|mainspring} provides additional tension to its outer part. That {color_blue|metal strip} really wants to snap back to its straight shape, so it pushes against the wall of the {color_red|barrel}, creating a lot of friction that keeps the mainspring in place:


[FIGURE 12 | mainspring_friction | ratio_100]

This locks the outer end of the {color_gray|mainspring} when the {color_yellow|arbor} moves the inner. If we were to keep winding the {color_gray|spring} past its maximum capacity, we’d overpower that friction letting the {color_gray|mainspring} slip inside – this acts as a safety mechanism to prevent the parts from breaking.

As we’ve seen, in its relaxed state, the {color_gray|mainspring} forms an S-shape with varied curvature throughout. This helps to balance the tension in {color_gray|mainspring’s} different sections when it is inside the {color_red|barrel}. Notice that the inner sections of the wound spring have a much smaller radius than the outer parts. If the relaxed spring was just a straight piece of metal, then after winding, the inner parts would be bent much more than the outer parts. With the S-shaped spring the outer sections of the spring are also under a similar tension because they want to get back to their curve that is bent in the opposite direction.

To secure the {color_gray|mainspring} and prevent dust from getting in we close the {color_red|barrel} with a lid that snaps into its place:


[FIGURE 13 | mainspring_lid | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | mainspring_lid_sl0]

We’ve managed to make some parts rotate and one could naively think that we could just attach a {color_hand|watch hand} to the {color_red|barrel} to make it track time. Unfortunately, that won’t really work – you can witness this in the demonstration below. You can see how this “watch” behaves after you wind the {color_gray|mainspring} with the slider and let it go:


[FIGURE 14 | mainspring_wind2 | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | mainspring_wind2_sl0]

We clearly have some work to do – the {color_hand|hand} spins way too fast and it only does a few rotations before the {color_gray|mainspring} inside the {color_red|barrel} runs out of the stored energy. Clearly, this contraption won’t let us track time in any reliable way.

If we wanted our watch to run continuously for around 40 hours on a single wind, we’d need the minute hand to complete 40 rotations in that time. Moreover, the second hand should cover around 40 × 60 = 2400 complete rotations in that time. We need to find a way to convert a small number of revolutions of the {color_red|barrel} into a large number of revolutions of the hands. This is where gears come in.


# Gears


I’ve talked about gears on this blog before, so let me just recap things very briefly. Gears can be used to change the speed of rotation between two different axes. In the demonstration below, you can witness that by observing little dots I put on each gear – the {color_yellow|yellow gear}, which is powered by the bigger {color_red|red gear}, takes much less time to finish a single revolution:


[FIGURE 15 | gears_base | ratio_100]

An important aspect of two matching gears is their number of teeth. Each tooth in one gear meets with a space between teeth in the other gear, so within a unit of time both gears rotate by the same number of teeth. If the number of teeth in two gears is different, those gears can take a different amount of time to complete a single rotation. In the demonstration below, you can change the ratio of the number of teeth between the driving {color_red|red gear} and the driven {color_yellow|yellow gear} to see how it affects the speed of rotation of that {color_yellow|yellow gear}:


[FIGURE 16 | gears_base2 | ratio_50]

[CONTROL | gears_base2_seg0]

These gears are intended to work with each other so the ratio of teeth is equivalent to the ratio of the gear radii. When the {color_red|driving gear} has more teeth than the {color_yellow|driven gear}, the {color_yellow|driven gear} makes more rotations than the {color_red|driving gear}. We can use this behavior to make the second hand of a watch rotate many times on a single rotation of the barrel.

Let’s consider how much of a speed increase we have to do here. The barrel can rotate close to 7 times on a single wind, but we want the second hand to complete around 2400 revolutions in the same time. We need the ratio of teeth, or the ratio of radii, to be around 343:1. Let’s see how that would look in practice. In the demonstration below, you can use the slider to look at the two gears from further away:


[FIGURE 17 | gears_base3 | ratio_50]

[CONTROL | gears_base3_sl0]

As you can see, these proportions are ridiculous – to make the {color_red|red gear} fit in any reasonably sized watch, the {color_yellow|yellow gear} would have to be absolutely tiny and both gears would have to have very fragile, microscopic teeth.

Instead, mechanical watches use a train of gears with multiple gears working in pairs – each pair increases the speed to some extent. In the demonstration below, you can see the four wheels participating in this reduction. Notice that there are two gears on most axes of rotation. You can control the speed of rotation of this gear train using the slider:


[FIGURE 18 | gear_train | ratio_50]

[CONTROL | gear_train_sl0]

The {color_red|barrel} acts as the {color_red|first wheel}, it drives the {color_orange|second wheel}, which drives the {color_yellow|third wheel}, which finally drives the {color_green|fourth wheel}. Notice that each big gear drives a smaller gear called a pinion. A pinion is mounted on the same shaft as the next big gear so we’re able to keep increasing the speed on each axis. This approach has significant advantages – we’re able to make the overall mechanism much smaller and we’ll soon use one of the intermediate wheels that rotates at a slower rate to drive minute and hour hands.

Before we finish up with gears, let me quickly mention the shape of their teeth. While many bigger machines use an involute shape for the profile of their gear teeth, mechanical watches commonly use cycloidal profiles which are obtained by rolling a circle on the surface of another circle.

Let’s see how the so-called going train that we’ve assembled works when we wind the {color_gray|mainspring} through the {color_yellow|arbor} and let the watch run:


[FIGURE 19 | gear_train2 | ratio_50]

[CONTROL | gear_train2_sl0]

We’ve certainly achieved the goal of the {color_hand|second hand} rotating many times on a single rotation of the {color_red|barrel}, but the speed of revolution of that {color_hand|hand} is still completely untamed. We need to find a way to control the rate of release of the energy stored in the {color_gray|mainspring} – we’ll do this with the escapement.


# Escapement


Let’s start by looking at the two components that create the escapement – the {color_cyan|escape wheel} and the {color_blue|pallet fork}:


[FIGURE 20 | escapement_parts | ratio_100]

Notice the unusual shape of the teeth of the {color_cyan|escape wheel} – it’s very different than the gears we’ve seen before. Its top part hosts a regularly shaped gear that can be used to turn that {color_cyan|wheel}.

The {color_blue|pallet fork} itself is made of metal, but notice the two {color_jewel|pinkish} transparent parts at its end. These are {color_jewel|jewels} made from synthetic ruby. That compound is not only very hard, which prevents its wear, but it also has a low coefficient of friction with steel. Let’s see why these properties are important by observing how these two components interact with each other:


[FIGURE 21 | escapement_action | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | escapement_action_sl0]

The {color_cyan|escape wheel} wants to rotate as indicated by the {color_red|red arrow}. The {color_blue|pallet fork} prevents that motion, but as we pivot that {color_blue|pallet fork} back and forth we let the {color_cyan|escape wheel} briefly escape from that jail only to be stopped again.

We’ll see the details of that interaction in a few paragraphs, but right now this mechanism lets us control the rotation of the {color_cyan|escape wheel} by simply moving the {color_blue|pallet fork} from one side to another. Let’s see how these pieces fit into the rest of the assembly. In the demonstration below, I’ve wound the spring for you so the {color_red|barrel}, through the gear train, ends up trying to rotate the {color_cyan|escape wheel}. Using the two buttons you can switch the position of the {color_blue|pallet fork}:


[FIGURE 22 | gear_train3 | ratio_50]

[CONTROL | gear_train3_seg0]

The mainspring wants to unwind by rotating the {color_cyan|escape wheel}, but the {color_blue|pallet fork} only allows this to happen for a brief period of time. Because of the gear reduction, the rotation of the {color_red|barrel} is pretty much invisible. However, if you observe the {color_hand|hand} attached to the {color_green|fourth wheel}, you can see it gently rotate as you swing the {color_blue|pallet fork} back and forth.

The little time keeping mechanism is almost fully functional now. The last remaining piece here is a device that will automatically tick the {color_blue|pallet fork} back and forth. However, for the watch to track time correctly that ticking action has to happen at an appropriate cadence. This is where the balance comes in – it forms the beating heart of a watch.


# Balance


Let’s bring up the first torsion spring we saw before – recall that once you twist it from its original position, it will oscillate back and forth, only to settle after a while:


[FIGURE 23 | torsion_spring2 | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | torsion_spring2_sl0]

We can control the rate of this periodic motion by adjusting two parameters. The first one is the stiffness of the spring, which primarily depends on its height, thickness, and length, as well as the type of material from which it’s made. The second one is the mass and its distribution, or, more precisely, the moment of inertia of the object that the spring rotates. Moment of inertia increases when more mass is put further away from the axis of rotation. In the demonstration below, you can tweak both the {color_yellow|stiffness} of the spring and {color_blue|moment of inertia} of the attached mass to see how these parameters affect the period of rotation:


[FIGURE 24 | torsion_spring3 | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | torsion_spring3_sl0]

[CONTROL | torsion_spring3_sl1]

[CONTROL | torsion_spring3_sl2]

By carefully tuning these parameters, we can make this system oscillate at a desired rate. This idea of using a torsion spring with attached mass is exactly what mechanical watches use as their source of precise time tracking. The balance is formed by the {color_violet|balance wheel} attached to the {color_gray|balance spring}. In this watch the {color_violet|balance wheel} oscillates back and forth at a fairly high frequency:


[FIGURE 25 | balance_wheel | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | balance_wheel_sl0]

At the bottom side of the balance wheel you’ll find another pinkish transparent jewel called {color_jewel|jewel roller}. While small, this part is very important – this {color_jewel|jewel} hits the other end of the {color_blue|pallet fork} as the {color_violet|balance wheel} rotates, which in turn pushes the {color_blue|pallet fork} back and forth. Let’s first look at an overview of how the {color_violet|balance wheel} interacts with the other parts. In the demonstration below, you can slow things down with the slider:


[FIGURE 26 | gear_train4 | ratio_40]

[CONTROL | gear_train4_sl0]

Let’s look at this interaction up close, as it deserves a closer attention. In the demonstration below, you can scrub back and forth in time to see all the action as it happens:


[FIGURE 27 | gear_train5 | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | gear_train5_sl0]

{gear_train5_explainer|{color_violet|balance wheel} is swinging back} {gear_train5_explainer|{color_jewel|jewel roller} strikes the {color_blue|pallet fork}, knocking it out of position} {gear_train5_explainer|{color_cyan|escape wheel} unlocks and pushes the {color_jewel|jewel} of the {color_blue|pallet fork}} {gear_train5_explainer|{color_blue|pallet fork} pushes the {color_jewel|jewel roller} and the {color_violet|balance wheel}} {gear_train5_explainer|{color_cyan|escape wheel} locks again} {gear_train5_explainer|{color_violet|balance wheel} continues its swing}

As the {color_violet|balance wheel} swings, the {color_jewel|jewel roller} strikes the {color_blue|pallet fork}, which unlocks the {color_cyan|escape wheel}. Once unlocked, the {color_cyan|escape wheel} powered by the mainspring pushes on the {color_blue|pallet fork} which, through the {color_jewel|jewel roller}, pushes on the {color_violet|balance wheel} itself. This causes the {color_violet|balance wheel} to gain some energy, which prevents it from stopping after a while – it’s equivalent to giving a push to a person swinging on a swing. When the {color_violet|balance wheel} comes back, it performs the same action, just in the other direction.

You may also have noticed a subtle dance between the {color_yellow|little horn} at the end of the {color_blue|pallet fork} and the {color_green|notched disk} on the {color_violet|balance wheel}. Those parts make sure that the {color_blue|pallet fork} can switch sides only at the appropriate time – it’s a safety mechanism that prevents the watch from locking up when the watch is shaken or dropped:


[FIGURE 28 | gear_train7 | ratio_100]

[CONTROL | gear_train7_sl0]

Once the {color_blue|pallet fork} unlocks the {color_cyan|escape wheel}, that wheel has to start spinning very quickly. This is why gears in the gear train have holes in them – it reduces their moment of inertia so that the {color_red|barrel} can accelerate them more quickly.

It’s also important to mention that the gear train not only increases the speed of the gears, but it also reduces the forces acting on the balance. The {color_red|barrel} itself turns quite forcefully but at the {color_cyan|escape wheel} the torque is greatly reduced, which prevents the {color_cyan|escape wheel} from pushing the {color_blue|pallet fork} and thus the {color_violet|balance wheel} with too much vigor.

Let’s look at the entirety of what we’ve built so far one last time. I’m now running the mechanism at its normal speed:


[FIGURE 29 | gear_train6 | ratio_50]

In this watch movement the {color_violet|balance wheel} does a full back and forth swing four times per second, hitting the {color_blue|pallet fork} twice during each cycle, for a total of 8 beats per second or 28,800 beats per hour. While different watches may have different rates, they all do a tiny turn of the {color_hand|second hand} many times per second, which gives mechanical watches the illusion of a very smooth hand motion.

In principle, all the pieces we have here are sufficient for the watch to run, but we’re still missing a few details. More importantly, we’ve just been hanging the parts in the air, so it’s time we started a proper assembly of the complete watch movement.

# Final Words


In the 1970s mechanical watches started to be dethroned by quartz models, which track time by electronically counting vibrations of a quartz crystal. As technology progressed, typical watches only increased their reliance on digital circuits. Modern smart reincarnations resemble their archetypes only in shape and placement on wrists.

Mechanical watches are not as accurate as digital ones. They require maintenance and are more fragile. Despite all these drawbacks, these devices show a true mastery of engineering. With creative use of miniature gears, levers, and springs, a mechanical watch rises from its dormant components to become truly alive.
